Topological Generation of the Acts

How one procedural system produces every act's spatial form — open field → canyon → holed cave → deep cave — and the routing, gating and commitment logic underneath it.

Status: 🟡 Early Concept Confidence: ★★★☆☆ Roadmap phase: 3 (post-MVP) Scope: Environment / level topology Date: 2026-07-11

What this document is. A conceptual design for the topology layer of procedural level generation — the spatial skeleton of an act and how the player moves and decides through it. It is written ahead of any engine work (Unity, when it comes) and deliberately stays at the level of model, rules and pipeline rather than implementation detail.

Where it sits. Procedural generation is a Phase 3 system; the MVP uses handcrafted arenas + a hub and explicitly excludes it. This doc exists so the eventual build starts from a decided model instead of a blank page. The art / dressing layer (making a generated canyon look like the concept art) and the dungeon map system are each their own future spec and are only referenced here as dependencies.

1 · The answer, up front

The founding question was: can a single generator span wide-open surface areas, canyons, caves with ceiling holes, and deep enclosed caves — or is that six unrelated generators?

One system does all of it, provided two things people usually tangle together are kept separate (§2), and provided the act's differences are expressed as parameters rather than as distinct algorithms (§3). Everything else in this doc — connectivity, routing, gating — is the logic layered on top of that spatial skeleton.

2 · Core principle: Floor ≠ Enclosure

Do not generate "a canyon" or "a cave" as a monolithic shape. Generate in two passes:

  1. Floor pass — the navigable play-space: a bounded footprint carrying the tactical features (chokepoints, ledges, cover, hazards, LOS blockers) and the connections to neighbouring spaces. This pass is nearly identical for every act. A fight floor is a fight floor.
  2. Enclosure pass — a shell wrapped around that floor: walls up the sides, ceiling overhead. This is where acts diverge, and it is almost the entire reason Act 1 feels open and Act 6 feels buried.
Why this matters

The split lines up exactly with the combat camera. Because the game is played on a pulled-back MMO / tactical camera, the environment has two different jobs: the floor is play-space (must stay readable) and the enclosure is backdrop (mostly seen, not walked — so it can carry all the painterly density of the concept art without ever having to stay combat-legible).

3 · Six acts are two knobs, not six shapes

Every act is a point in a 2-D space defined by lateral enclosure (how tall/closed the side walls are) and overhead enclosure (how much ceiling exists), plus a boundary treatment and a light source drawn from the Visual Language Guide.

Read down the ceiling column: 0% → open strip → punctured → 100%. That single ramp is the entire surface-to-abyss descent. The alarming jump from "open meadow" to "deep cave" is really just ceiling coverage climbing from 0 to 100 and the boundary shifting soft → hard → membrane. The floor generator underneath barely changes.

Three parts that are genuinely non-trivial

  • Act 1's "open" still needs a boundary. Open ≠ unbounded — you cannot let players walk to infinity. It means the boundary is disguised: receding hills, a treeline, a river, a fog death-zone. "No walls" is really "soft walls you don't read as walls."
  • Act 3's ceiling holes are a first-class generator feature, not decoration. The god-ray shafts are the whole identity of that act; the generator must punch real gaps in the ceiling shell where the light beams will land dramatically on the floor.
  • Transition acts want an enclosure gradient — a single space can ramp from open-mouth (entrance) to closed (deeper), which is how you honour the Visual Guide's Transition Lock and avoid a hard visual cut between acts.

4 · Spatial character: readable pockets, organic traversal

Reference point: WoW's Wailing Caverns — the archetype for Act 4 (Living Depths) and proof that a pulled-back MMO camera can carry vertical, mazy caves. The lesson from dissecting it:

The Wailing Caverns rule

Its verticality and maze-ness live almost entirely in the traversal — the winding stuff you walk through between fights. The actual pulls and boss fights happen in readable pockets and grottos, widenings where the floor flattens and the encounter is legible. So: spend the verticality / overgrowth / maze budget on the connective tissue; keep combat floors near-single-plane and clear.

What not to inherit: Wailing Caverns' signature is that everyone gets lost — which directly violates the Bible's "players should always understand their objective / readability above complexity." Borrow its grotto-and-tunnel rhythm, verticality and living overgrowth; do not borrow its disorienting loop-maze. Readable critical path + optional loops.

5 · Two graphs: Mission over Space

Never conflate "how it's connected" with "how you progress." The generator maintains two graphs:

Mission graph

The intended progression: entrance → fork → objective → gate → … → boss. Directional, gated, readable. A mostly-linear DAG with branches that rejoin. This is where "linear descent with pathing choices" lives.

Space graph

The physical connectivity: how chambers and tunnels actually wire together — including loops, shortcuts and dead-end spurs that are not part of the critical path. This is where the maze lives.

The key move: the space graph can be as cyclic and mazy as Wailing Caverns while the mission graph stays a clean directed descent. The loops are physical, not logical — you get "this tunnel loops back to that gate I passed" without ever confusing which way is forward.

Method — this has a name

What we want is cyclic dungeon generation (Joris Dormans / the Unexplored games). Standard generators grow a tree — which is why so many roguelikes feel like sterile branching corridors with loops bolted on. Cyclic generation uses the loop itself as the building block: a cycle with a "there" path and a "back / shortcut" path, usually with a lock on one side. Chain and nest those along the descent axis and shortcuts, "I've been here from the other side," and understood-before-found locks emerge naturally, while progression stays legible.

6 · Routing & commitment engine

This is the spine of the design and resolves the central tension: the party should move freely (good feel) yet their routing decisions should stick (meaningful choice).

The tension

Two decisions constrain the solution. Traversal is two-way (you can double back through the knot — needed so the party can scout options before committing). And regeneration is generous: health and mana refill quickly between fights, because waiting on regen is dead time and feels bad. Together these remove attrition as a commitment engine — if you're always topped up and can walk anywhere, you'd simply take everything and no routing decision would bite.

So commitment must be structural, not resource-based.

The model: two-way pods, one-way branching descents

  • Within a pod (a region of the act): fully two-way, free, scoutable cyclic maze — the Wailing Caverns knot, no attrition, deliberate as long as you like.
  • Between pods: a one-way descent (the "ledge you jump down"). Once you drop, the pod above is sealed — not as punishment, but because descending is the commitment. You had full freedom to deliberate first; the drop is you calling it.

The elegant collapse: the branching drop is the decision

Slay-the-Spire's "elite or trader?" needs no lock — it can simply be which ledge you jump down. The pod above lets the party scout/preview both branches; they deliberate; they pick a ledge and commit. The commitment device and the decision are the same event — and "drop down a ledge to choose your path" is thematically perfect for a descent game. Slay the Spire's fork, made diegetic and vertical.

This also auto-scopes backtracking: you only ever re-walk the current pod, never the whole act — keeping the space legible and stopping content-hoovering.

Two commitment tools, two scales

  • Branching one-way descents → forward routing ("which way down — elite or trader?"). The everyday decision pulse.
  • Key-gated exclusive doors (§7) → optional in-pod rewards ("one key up here — vault or shrine?"). The combinatorial "can't have both."
  • Two-way shortcuts → survive, now scoped inside a pod (open a shortcut so you don't re-walk the maze / get back to a well fast).
Two rules that fall out (generator responsibilities)

1 · No blind drops. The one-way descent is placed after the party can scout/preview both branches, or the choice isn't informed. (This is where the deferred map system plugs in.)
2 · The gate is abstract, skinned per act. The generator emits an abstract commitment gate; each act renders it — a slope into the canyon mouth (Act 1), a cliff ledge (Act 2), a waterfall or chasm (Acts 3–6), a collapsing span. "Ledge drop" is the cave-native skin, not the only one.

7 · Gating: lock & key

Alongside structural commitment (§6), optional content is gated by lock and key. The important property: irreversibility here is combinatorial, not temporal — the design says "you can't have both," never "you can't go back."

  • Fungible single-use keys. Any key fits any lock of its tier and is consumed on use — so the tension is purely about where to spend, never about holding the wrong colour. A few special keys may exist for special locks (a boss gate, a god-altar).
  • The exclusive-door primitive. A cycle carrying one key and two mutually-exclusive locks. Two-way traversal lets the party scout both doors before committing the key; they claim exactly one.
The one number that is the whole thing

keys < openable locks. If the party can open everything, there is no decision. The design target is scarcity — "you'll find 3 keys this act, but there are 7 doors worth opening." Every key spent is opportunity cost. That single ratio is the dial that makes routing matter.

The generator's solvability contract

  • Obligation: every mandatory lock on the descent spine must always have a key reachable upstream of it. The critical path can never soft-lock.
  • Freedom: optional and exclusive locks are explicitly allowed to stay shut forever. That isn't a bug — spending your key on door A is supposed to leave B locked this run.

8 · Rewards early, routes at boundaries — and the map falls out for free

What sits behind an exclusive door is weighted by depth:

  • Rewards, weighted early — door A = a Relic, door B = an item cache / gold / a Well. Cheap to author, frequent small decisions, feeds build divergence.
  • Routes, weighted at act boundaries — door A and door B are different paths through / into the next act: different encounters, sub-biome, difficulty, length. Bigger, replayable divergence, without asking the generator to build two full alternate acts every time.
Unifying insight

Act-boundary route-doors are the run map. You don't need a separate abstract map screen (Slay the Spire nodes, FTL beacons) — you choose your macro path by walking through a door. This diegetic branching keeps the player in the world and lets the same lock-and-key vocabulary carry both the micro decisions (reward-doors inside an act) and the macro decisions (route-doors between acts). One system, two scales.

Dependency — the dungeon map (deferred to its own spec). A progressively-revealed dungeon map is what makes both the micro and macro choices informed. Architecturally it costs almost nothing: the map is just a filtered, progressively-unfogged render of the space graph this doc already produces. No new data structure. The reveal-trigger design (fog baseline vs placed vantage/threshold reveals) and reveal-depth (topology + node types, contents hidden) belong in that separate document; noted here only so the generator remembers to emit map-ready graph data and to place reveals upstream of decisions.

9 · Generation pipeline

The order the system runs. Each stage consumes the previous stage's output; the last two stages are the two passes from §2.

  1. Mission graph — lay down the linear-ish gated descent with its fork choices.
  2. Cyclic space graph — realise the mission graph physically using cyclic generation: pods, loops, shortcuts, spurs.
  3. Commitment & lock/key placement — place one-way descents (with their branch = routing choice) and lock/key pairs; run the solvability check.
  4. Node typing — assign each pocket a type: combat, elite, trader, treasure, shrine/altar, event, rest, boss. Decision-bearing — this is what the routing choices route toward and what the map surfaces.
  5. Spatial embedding — turn the abstract graph into laid-out 3-D volumes (pockets + connecting traversal), respecting the 2.5-D constraint (single navigable floor layer; verticality lives in traversal).
  6. Floor pass — generate each pocket's readable play-space and tactical features.
  7. Enclosure pass — wrap the act-specific shell (walls + ceiling) per the §3 knobs; punch Act-3 skylights; apply enclosure gradients at transitions.
  8. Dressing pass — (separate art spec) place set-pieces, props, vegetation, palette and lighting so it reads as the concept art.

10 · Open knobs & deferred dependencies

Open tuning knobs

  • Key economy — the keys-to-locks ratio per act.
  • Loop / shortcut density — subtle vs heavy Wailing-Caverns knot.
  • Reward-vs-route weighting curve across the act depth.
  • Pod size & count per act; descent cadence.
  • How far spatial embedding goes (grammar-driven vs hand-tuned).

Deferred to their own specs

  • The dungeon map — reveal triggers & depth.
  • Art / dressing layer — hitting the concept-art look.
  • Acts-vs-Gods axis — do the 6 descent acts and the 5 God/Colour biomes nest, or is one the biome driver? Affects how many enclosure/kit templates the system needs.
  • Encounter population — enemy composition per typed node (Bible ch. 11–12).

11 · Glossary

  • Floor / Enclosure — the two generation passes: navigable play-space vs the wall+ceiling shell wrapped around it.
  • Pod — a region of an act: a self-contained two-way cyclic maze the party explores freely.
  • Commitment gate / descent — a one-way transition between pods; its branch is a routing choice. Abstract; skinned per act (ledge, waterfall, chasm, slope).
  • Mission graph / Space graph — logical progression vs physical connectivity; the former directed & readable, the latter cyclic & mazy.
  • Cyclic dungeon generation — generation method using loops (not trees) as the primitive.
  • Exclusive-door primitive — one key + two mutually-exclusive locks on a scoutable loop.
  • Combinatorial irreversibility — "you can't have both," as opposed to temporal "you can't go back."
  • Diegetic branching — choosing the macro route by walking through a door rather than via an abstract map screen.

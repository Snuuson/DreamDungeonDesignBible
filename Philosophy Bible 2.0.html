Game Philosophy Bible

The identity, principles, systems, world, and development philosophy guiding DreamDungeon.

Version 0.3 Draft22 Chapters6 PartsContains internal narrative truth

Purpose of This Bible

DreamDungeon is a long-term project whose mechanics, values, content, and production priorities will continue to evolve through prototyping and playtesting. This Bible preserves the identity of the game throughout that process.

It defines why DreamDungeon exists in its current form, which experiences it aims to create, and which principles should guide future decisions. It is not intended to provide exhaustive implementation specifications or balance values. Those belong in the Technical Game Design Document and the System Bibles.

This Bible serves four purposes:

  • Preserve the identity of DreamDungeon throughout development.
  • Give designers, developers, artists, and other collaborators a shared design language.
  • Explain the reasoning behind major design decisions.
  • Provide a stable reference against which new features can be evaluated.

When a proposed feature conflicts with this Bible, the team should either revise the feature or make a deliberate, recorded decision to update the philosophy. The Bible should evolve when the project's identity genuinely changes, but it should not change merely because an isolated implementation changes.

Canonical Document Boundaries

DreamDungeon separates its knowledge into several document types:

  • The Game Philosophy Bible defines the project's identity, intended experience, and design reasoning.
  • The Technical GDD defines current system behavior, implementation rules, configurable values, and edge cases.
  • The System Bibles contain frequently changing structured content such as Spells, Conditions, Talents, Equipment, Enemies, and Bosses.
  • The Glossary contains canonical definitions and approved terminology.
  • The Roadmap, Open Questions, and Future Ideas are separate living documents that change more frequently than this Bible.

Detailed information should have one canonical home. Other documents may summarize it for context, but should link or refer to the canonical source rather than creating a second competing definition.

Status Vocabulary

  • Stable — A foundational principle expected to change rarely.
  • Prototype — A principle or system currently being validated and expected to evolve.
  • Early Concept — A direction that has not yet been sufficiently developed or tested.
  • Living — Material intentionally updated throughout development.
  • Deprecated — Material retained for historical understanding but no longer current.

Narrative Perspective Vocabulary

The world and story intentionally contain conflicting perspectives. Narrative chapters distinguish among:

  • Objective Truth — Internal designer knowledge about what actually happened.
  • World Belief — What religions, cultures, scholars, and ordinary inhabitants generally believe.
  • Player Knowledge — What the player is expected to understand at a particular point in the experience.

These perspectives must not be blended. In particular, early player-facing material must not reveal that the characters are dreamwalking.

Chapter 1 — Vision & Design Philosophy

StatusStable

1.1 What Is DreamDungeon?#

DreamDungeon is a cooperative tactical action roguelike in which a party fights through increasingly dangerous Encounters while gradually constructing unique Builds through drafted Spells, Equipment, and Talents.

The game is not intended to be a rapid hack-and-slash experience in which success depends primarily on reflexes or mechanical execution. DreamDungeon rewards observation, communication, planning, positioning, and adaptation. Combat should remain responsive and satisfying, but mechanical speed alone should never replace tactical understanding.

Players do not select rigid classes before a run. Each character begins from a clean foundation and gradually develops through shared opportunities and changing constraints. Combinations of Spells, Equipment, Talents, and party needs create emergent roles such as Tank, Healer, Damage Dealer, Support, and Controller.

A run should feel less like selecting a finished identity and more like discovering what a character becomes through the decisions made during the adventure.

1.2 The Core Experience#

DreamDungeon is built around one central fantasy:

Overcome dangerous Encounters through teamwork, planning, and creative Builds.

Every major system should reinforce this fantasy:

  • Combat should feel responsive, deliberate, and satisfying.
  • Encounters should encourage communication and tactical adaptation.
  • Spell Drafts should create meaningful individual and party decisions.
  • Progression should reward experimentation rather than predetermined optimization.
  • Equipment distribution should encourage cooperation.
  • Victory should feel earned through understanding and teamwork rather than statistics alone.

When players finish a successful run, they should remember the situations they solved together, the Builds they discovered, and the decisions that saved the party—not only the damage numbers they produced.

1.3 A Hardcore Cooperative Adventure#

DreamDungeon is intended to feel like a long, high-stakes cooperative adventure. A successful run may last several hours and may continue across multiple play sessions. Saving and resuming an active run is therefore part of the core vision rather than an optional convenience.

Permadeath gives a run emotional weight. Players should become attached to the character and Build that developed during the adventure. Losing that character should matter, but the loss should also create excitement about discovering a different Build with the group in the next run.

The word hardcore describes this combination of commitment, permadeath, and meaningful loss. It does not mean that the game should rely on unexplained punishment, unreadable mechanics, or extreme mechanical execution.

1.4 The Long-Term Vision#

The complete game is envisioned as a cooperative roguelike built around tactical combat, branching exploration, temporary character progression, and high replayability.

The final journey contains six Acts. Each Act contains one Dungeon, and each Dungeon contains multiple branching Sections. Players choose routes, complete Encounters, discover Points of Interest, acquire rewards, and gradually shape their Builds while descending deeper into the Dream Dungeon.

Different Sections may be neutral, influenced by one or more Color Schools, focused on Points of Interest, or built around major combat challenges. Biomes, Enemies, mechanics, and environmental storytelling should make each part of the descent distinct while preserving one coherent world.

Meaningful variation is more important than unlimited randomness. Random systems should generate interesting decisions, not arbitrary outcomes that invalidate planning.

1.5 Progression Between Runs#

No direct character power carries from one run into the next. Each run begins from a clean gameplay state.

Player knowledge is the primary form of progression between runs. Players gradually learn systems, Enemy behavior, Encounter patterns, interactions, and strategic possibilities. This knowledge makes the group more capable without weakening the importance of a fresh run.

Non-power progression may persist when it does not undermine that principle. Examples may include discovered information, narrative revelations, records, cosmetics, difficulty options, or other forms of recognition and presentation. These systems must not provide direct statistical power to future characters.

1.6 Design Philosophy#

Responsive Combat#

Player actions should produce immediate, understandable feedback. Input, animation, effects, sound, and results should maintain a strong connection. Responsiveness is more important than realism.

DreamDungeon draws inspiration from the combat flow of games such as World of Warcraft, Guild Wars 2, and Dota while pursuing its own cooperative roguelike structure. These inspirations are reference points, not implementation requirements.

Tactical Cooperation and Communication#

The game should naturally encourage players to communicate before and during Encounters. Cooperation should emerge because coordination produces better outcomes, not because players are forced through arbitrary multiplayer gates.

Spell allocation, Equipment distribution, target priority, positioning, Crowd Control, healing, Threat management, and route decisions should all create reasons to discuss strategy.

Meaningful Build Decisions#

Players should not be overwhelmed with constant upgrades or inconsequential choices. Spell Drafts, Equipment, and Talents should matter because their value depends on the current Build, party composition, available resources, and upcoming challenges.

Easy to Learn, Hard to Master#

Individual systems should remain approachable. Complexity should emerge through interactions among clear systems rather than through increasingly complicated isolated rules.

1.7 Design Principles#

Simple Spells. Deep Builds.#

Individual Spells should be intuitive and independently useful. Depth emerges from combinations of Spells, Conditions, Talents, Equipment, party composition, and player decisions.

Simple Enemies. Deep Encounters.#

Most Enemies should have clear battlefield roles and a limited number of understandable capabilities. Difficulty and variety emerge through Enemy combinations, behavior, terrain, and Encounter composition.

Simple Encounters. Deep Teamwork.#

Encounter objectives and threats should remain understandable. Success comes from communication, positioning, target priority, battlefield control, resource management, and adaptation.

Readability Above Complexity#

Players should understand what is happening and why. Animations, silhouettes, effects, sound, interfaces, and combat feedback should communicate mechanics without overwhelming the player.

Cooperation Over Individual Performance#

Systems should reward players for supporting one another. The party's survival and combined Build matter more than individual performance statistics.

Randomness Creates Variety, Not Frustration#

Randomness should create new decisions and replayability. It should not regularly invalidate planning, make a run unwinnable, or require one specific reward to succeed.

1.8 What DreamDungeon Is Not#

DreamDungeon is intentionally not:

  • A class-based RPG with permanently fixed character identities.
  • A hack-and-slash game focused primarily on action speed.
  • A game in which complexity comes mainly from long Spell descriptions.
  • A game in which success requires obtaining one exact Build.
  • A game in which Enemies become difficult only through increased Health and damage.
  • A game in which randomness routinely invalidates player decisions.
  • A game with a separate endgame that replaces its core loop. A future Endless Mode may extend that loop, but the route itself remains the goal.

Chapter 2 — Core Gameplay Loop

StatusPrototype

2.1 Purpose#

The Core Gameplay Loop is the backbone of DreamDungeon. Every system should reinforce this loop rather than compete with it. Players should understand their immediate objective, what they are working toward, and why decisions between Encounters matter.

This chapter describes the player journey. Exact reward formulas, timings, interaction rules, and failure handling belong in the Technical GDD.

2.2 The Long-Term Gameplay Loop#

In the final game, players descend through six Acts. Each Act contains one Dungeon made from multiple branching Sections.

The long-term loop is:

  1. Begin or resume a run with the party.
  2. Enter an Act and its Dungeon.
  3. Choose among available branching Sections.
  4. Explore the selected route and discover its Combat Areas and Points of Interest.
  5. Complete Encounters and other challenges.
  6. Gain Experience, Gold, Equipment, Relics, and other appropriate rewards.
  7. Develop individual Builds through Spell Drafts, Equipment, and Talents.
  8. Prepare for increasingly dangerous Sections and milestone Encounters.
  9. Defeat the Dungeon's major challenge and continue into the next Act.
  10. Complete the final Act or lose the run through party defeat.

Route choices should create meaningful tradeoffs. The final game may use blocked paths and points of no return so that the party cannot collect every reward in one run. Choosing what to leave behind is part of the strategy.

2.3 Encounter Rhythm#

An Encounter is an authored development record that groups one or more Enemy Groups into an intended tactical challenge within a Section. It may reference the Combat Areas, environmental challenges, completion conditions and rewards designed for that challenge, but it does not create a hard spatial or gameplay boundary. Where the level permits, players may combine groups, disengage or skip an Encounter.

A Section may contain multiple Encounters. This allows a Section to create a broader rhythm of exploration, combat, preparation, and reward without making Section, Combat Area, Enemy Group, and Encounter interchangeable terms.

2.4 The MVP Gameplay Loop#

The MVP removes branching exploration so the team can isolate and test Combat, Encounters, cooperation, and progression.

The Central Hub is an MVP-only preparation space. The Combat Arena is an MVP-only level structure containing the Combat Area used for the current sequence of Encounters.

The MVP loop is:

  1. Begin or resume the run.
  2. Prepare in the MVP-only Central Hub.
  3. Select starting Spells and equip starting Equipment when applicable.
  4. Complete the Ready Check.
  5. Enter the Combat Arena and complete the next Encounter.
  6. Gain shared Experience and appropriate Gold and Equipment rewards.
  7. Complete a shared Spell Draft when the party reaches the required level.
  8. Return to the MVP-only Central Hub.
  9. Heal, trade, shop, review the party, and adjust the active Build.
  10. Repeat until the End Boss is defeated or the party loses the run.

Encounters 5, 10, and 15 contain Mini Bosses. Encounter 20 contains the End Boss of the MVP.

2.5 Between Encounters#

Preparation spaces should create room for discussion. In the MVP-only Central Hub, players may recover at the Well, purchase Equipment from the Shop, trade Equipment, review their Builds, manage equipped Spells, and prepare for the next Encounter.

The calm preparation phase exists to support cooperation. It should not become so long or complicated that it breaks the rhythm of the run.

2.6 Build Evolution#

A Build develops gradually:

  • Spells define what a player can actively do.
  • Equipment determines much of the character's statistical power.
  • Talents connect mechanics and strengthen the identity of the developing Build.

Early progression establishes core capabilities. Mid-run progression builds synergy and role identity. Later progression refines and strengthens the completed Build without making early decisions obsolete.

2.7 Intentionally Postponed Systems#

The MVP postpones systems that are not required to validate the core loop, including full procedural exploration, branching Dungeons, complete narrative delivery, advanced itemization, Spell Ranks, and Spell Evolution.

Postponement does not reject the long-term vision. It protects development focus until the foundation has proven enjoyable.

Chapter 3 — Combat

StatusPrototype

3.1 Combat Philosophy#

Combat is the foundation on which every other gameplay system is built. Progression, Spell Drafts, Talents, Equipment, Enemies, and Encounters exist to create more interesting combat decisions.

The objective is not to create the most mechanically demanding combat system. DreamDungeon should reward planning, adaptation, timing, positioning, communication, and knowledge. Moment-to-moment play should remain understandable while mastery emerges through the relationships among these skills.

Whenever a combat feature is proposed, it should answer one question:

Does this create more meaningful decisions during combat?

If the answer is no, the feature should be reconsidered.

3.2 Intended Combat Feel#

Combat should feel:

  • Responsive
  • Chunky
  • Direct
  • Readable
  • Reactive
  • Tactical

Actions should feel impactful. Clear animation, sound, visual effects, hit reactions, telegraphs, and interface feedback matter more than excessive spectacle.

3.3 Responsive Tactical Action#

Combat combines free movement and a controllable camera with hotbar-based actions, auto attacks, cast timing, positioning, and tactical target selection. Tab targeting provides a dependable targeting foundation, while ground-targeted, directional, and telegraphed actions preserve spatial decision-making.

Movement and animation should support deliberate decisions without making the character feel unresponsive. Exact casting models, animation rules, weapon behavior, and input handling are defined in the Technical GDD.

3.4 Party Roles#

DreamDungeon has no predefined classes. Roles emerge from the interaction of drafted Spells, Equipment, Talents, and party needs.

Common roles include:

  • Damage Dealer
  • Tank
  • Healer
  • Support
  • Controller
  • Hybrid combinations

Roles should help the party communicate, but should not become rigid restrictions. A player's role may change as the Build develops.

3.5 Cooperation#

Combat should create recurring opportunities for players to help one another through:

  • Positioning
  • Target priority
  • Crowd Control
  • Healing and mitigation
  • Buffs and debuffs
  • Threat management
  • Interrupts
  • Defensive timing
  • Resource and cooldown coordination

Cooperation should improve the party's chance of success without requiring one exact composition.

3.6 Threat#

Threat enables Tank-oriented gameplay by allowing players to control Enemy attention. Normal Threat behavior should be predictable and dependable when used correctly.

Selected Enemy abilities or Encounter rules may override normal Threat behavior to challenge other roles. Such exceptions must be intentional and clearly communicated. Unexplained randomness should not undermine the Tank's tools.

3.7 Crowd Control#

Crowd Control helps the party manage dangerous situations and should become increasingly valuable as Encounters become more demanding. It should provide a strong tactical advantage without requiring every party to use one exact control package.

Soft Crowd Control limits part of a unit's behavior. Hard Crowd Control temporarily prevents meaningful action. Hard Crowd Control should generally involve setup, shorter combat duration, diminishing returns, immunity rules, or explicit exceptions for major Enemies.

Exact Conditions, durations, and immunity rules belong in the Condition Bible and Technical GDD.

3.8 Combat Resources#

Momentum and Weakened create setup-and-payoff interactions across Spells, Conditions, and party play. Both count as Conditions for system interaction and carry the special Combat Resource tag.

Momentum is held and managed by a character. Weakened is applied to Enemies. Their exact generation, refresh, consumption, and numerical rules belong in the Technical GDD and Condition Bible.

Chapter 4 — Progression

StatusPrototype

4.1 Progression Philosophy#

Progression should provide meaningful choices rather than constant rewards. A run should feel like gradually discovering a character, not following a predetermined class progression.

Every progression system has a distinct primary purpose:

  • Spells define what the character can do.
  • Equipment provides much of the character's numerical power.
  • Talents connect mechanics and strengthen Build identity.

The systems may interact, but unnecessary overlap should be avoided so that each reward remains understandable and valuable.

4.2 Shared Party Growth#

Experience is shared so the party levels together. Shared progression reduces competition for advancement and allows players to focus on solving Encounters as a group.

Surviving an Encounter matters. A player death creates a progression consequence, reinforcing the value of protection and coordinated play without immediately ending the run. The exact Experience penalty remains a prototype balance rule in the Technical GDD.

A full party defeat ends the run.

4.3 Spell Drafts#

Spell Drafts are the primary source of active player capabilities and occur through party progression. The Draft is shared, so players must discuss how to allocate limited opportunities across the current party.

The size of the shared Spell offering scales with party size so every Draft creates meaningful allocation decisions. Exact offering counts belong in the Technical GDD.

Each offered Spell may be selected only once. Two players cannot own the same Spell simultaneously, and one player cannot own two copies of the same Spell.

Duplicate Spells are reserved for a future Rank system. A future selection of a duplicate may create a Rank Up or another explicitly designed outcome, but it must never result in owning two copies.

4.4 Spell Book#

Drafted Spells that are not currently equipped remain in the Spell Book. This allows players to adapt their active loadout during preparation without discarding the strategic consequences of earlier Draft choices.

Future systems may allow unwanted Spells to be sacrificed or otherwise repurposed. Such systems remain outside the current foundation.

4.5 Equipment Progression#

Equipment increases Character Statistics and therefore improves the effectiveness of auto attacks, Spells, defenses, Health, Mana, and other scalable mechanics.

Equipment is individually held and equipped. In the MVP, it can be freely traded during preparation. In the finished game, Equipment should be tradable anywhere outside combat. Distribution should encourage discussion about what most benefits the party rather than automatic personal ownership of every drop.

4.6 Talent Progression#

Talents strengthen and connect mechanics already present in the Build. They should usually change relationships and reinforce playstyles rather than merely provide raw numerical increases.

Talents belong to the complete MVP scope but may be implemented after the first playable Combat, Spell, and Equipment milestone.

4.7 Progression Between Runs#

All direct character power is temporary and ends with the run. The player retains knowledge and may retain non-power records, discoveries, cosmetics, or narrative progress when those systems do not weaken the fresh-start principle.

Chapter 5 — Resources

StatusPrototype

5.1 Resource Philosophy#

Resources should create meaningful decisions during or between Encounters. They are not intended to slow players down merely for the sake of restriction.

Every resource should answer a distinct gameplay question. A new resource should not be added when an existing system can express the same decision clearly.

5.2 Health#

Health is the primary survival resource. It answers: Can I survive?

Health should be threatened through Enemy actions, Encounter mechanics, positioning mistakes, and failures of coordination rather than unexplained unavoidable damage. Losing Health should generally feel like the consequence of an understandable event.

Health can be restored through healing, regeneration, and other defensive systems. The MVP uses the Well in the MVP-only Central Hub for accelerated recovery. The final game may use supplies such as rations for recovery between Encounters.

5.3 Mana#

Mana is the universal resource used for Spell casting. It answers: Can I continue casting?

Mana management should matter without becoming unnecessarily frustrating. Magical, healing, physical, melee, and ranged Builds may rely on Mana to different degrees. Costs, regeneration, Equipment, and efficient Spell use should influence long-term combat performance.

5.4 Momentum#

Momentum is a character-held Condition with the Combat Resource tag. It answers: How does my active combat rhythm build and change?

Selected actions generate or interact with Momentum. Some effects become stronger when Momentum is high, while others may consume it. Momentum should support multiple playstyles without becoming mandatory for every Build.

Exact stack limits, generation rules, decay, and consumption belong in the Condition Bible and Technical GDD.

5.5 Weakened#

Weakened is a single stack-based Condition applied to Enemies and carries the Combat Resource tag. It answers: How does the party prepare an Enemy for a control or payoff effect?

Applying Weakened to an unaffected Enemy creates the stack. Additional applications may refresh it. Selected abilities may consume the stack to enable or improve Crowd Control and other explicitly defined effects.

Exact durations, valid targets, refresh behavior, and consumption effects belong in the Condition Bible and Technical GDD.

5.6 Threat#

Threat is the combat value that influences which player an Enemy prefers to attack. It answers: Who controls Enemy attention?

Threat enables Tank-oriented Builds and should be predictable under normal rules. Explicit Enemy and Encounter mechanics may override it when clearly communicated.

5.7 Experience#

Experience represents party growth during a run. It answers: How does the party progress?

Experience is shared, allowing the party to level together and reach Spell Drafts at the same time. Player deaths reduce the Encounter's progression reward according to prototype rules defined in the Technical GDD.

In the finished game, different branches may offer different total Experience. Route choice can therefore affect how quickly the party progresses, while exact values remain a Technical GDD concern.

5.8 Gold#

Gold is the primary purchasing currency during a run. It answers: Which opportunities can a player choose to purchase?

Gold drops are divided among eligible players. Each player then owns and spends an individual balance. The Shop is Gold's primary MVP use and complements random Equipment drops by providing controlled purchasing opportunities. In the finished game, other Points of Interest may also accept Gold as the run's main currency.

5.9 Relics#

Relics are milestone rewards used for Talent progression. They answer: How does a player invest in the deeper identity of a Build?

Every MVP Mini Boss awards Relics. The MVP End Boss does not award a Relic because defeating it ends the run. In the finished game, Act-ending Bosses may award Relics when progression continues afterward. Relics are awarded to and spent by individual players, even when all eligible players receive them from the same milestone. Exact quantities and Talent-offering rules remain implementation variables.

5.10 Resource Relationships#

The resource structure remains readable because each resource has a distinct purpose:

  • Health — survival
  • Mana — continued Spell use
  • Momentum — active combat rhythm
  • Weakened — Enemy setup and payoff
  • Threat — control of Enemy attention
  • Experience — shared party growth
  • Gold — individual purchasing power
  • Relics — individual Talent investment

Chapter 6 — Spell Design

StatusPrototype

6.1 Purpose#

Spells are the primary way players actively interact with combat. They define what a character can do and form the foundation of every Build.

Individual Spell entries, values, targeting data, and implementation status belong in the Spell Bible.

6.2 Simple Spells. Deep Builds.#

Individual Spells should be intuitive, readable, and immediately useful. Complexity should emerge from Conditions, Talents, Equipment, party composition, Enemy behavior, and Encounter design rather than lengthy isolated Spell rules.

6.3 Independent Value and Synergy#

Every Spell should be worth drafting on its own. A Spell may become stronger or more interesting through synergy, but a Build should not depend on finding one exact partner Spell to make an earlier choice functional.

Some overlap is intentional. Multiple Spells may address similar needs in different ways so that random Drafts continue to offer viable options.

Conditions, Talents, and party cooperation should create most cross-system interactions. Long mandatory combo chains should generally be avoided.

6.4 Build and Role Diversity#

Because DreamDungeon has no fixed classes, combinations of Spells gradually shape the player's role. Different selections should naturally support Damage, Tanking, Healing, Utility, Crowd Control, Mobility, Support, and hybrid approaches.

Every Spell should have a recognizable primary purpose. Secondary functions are welcome when they remain understandable.

6.5 Scaling Philosophy#

Spells gain much of their numerical power through Character Statistics provided by Equipment. Talents primarily modify interactions and behavior, using raw numerical bonuses more selectively.

Spell Ranks are a future progression possibility. They must not be implemented as duplicate Spell ownership.

6.6 Draft Uniqueness#

The shared Spell Draft turns acquisition into a cooperative decision. A Spell can be selected only once, and the same Spell cannot be owned by multiple players simultaneously.

This limitation should encourage discussion about party needs without making one exact allocation necessary for success.

6.7 Readability#

Spell descriptions should remain concise and use approved Glossary terms. Icons, Color, animation, audio, targeting previews, and other presentation should communicate behavior whenever possible.

New keywords should be introduced only when they express reusable gameplay that cannot be communicated clearly through existing language.

Chapter 7 — Color Schools

StatusPrototype

7.1 Purpose#

The five Color Schools provide thematic identity, guide Spell fantasy, connect gameplay to the Five Old Gods, and encourage diverse Builds without functioning as traditional classes.

7.2 Colors Are Fantasies, Not Classes#

Every Color should feel distinct and should contribute useful tools to multiple party roles. A Color is not required to support every role equally well or function as a complete class by itself.

Strengths and weaknesses protect identity and encourage mixing Colors. Role overlap prevents Colors from becoming rigid class labels.

7.3 The Five Colors#

Red#

Red represents aggression, Momentum, weapon mastery, elemental combat, and enhancement.

Blue#

Blue represents arcane power, manipulation, control, utility, and reality-bending effects.

Green#

Green represents nature, restoration, mobility, preparation, ranged combat, and precision.

Black#

Black represents curses, corruption, assassination, sacrifice, summoning, and death as a resource or theme.

White#

White represents protection, healing, mitigation, summoning, blessing, and survival.

7.4 Identity and Mechanical Language#

Each Color should have recognizable visuals, themes, strengths, weaknesses, recurring Conditions, and mechanical patterns. Players should understand the fantasy of a Color before memorizing individual Spells.

Color-specific keywords should strengthen identity without preventing cross-Color interaction. Conditions may appear more frequently in one Color while remaining part of a shared system language.

7.5 The Five Old Gods#

Each Color is associated with one of the Five Old Gods. These connections influence Spells, Altars, Relics, Talents, Biomes, aesthetics, and worldbuilding.

The gods provide thematic foundations rather than dictating fixed player roles.

7.6 MVP Scope#

The exact number and selection of Color Schools used in the MVP remain an Open Variable. The MVP should include only the set needed to validate Color identity, Spell Drafting, Build diversity, and party interaction.

Chapter 8 — Conditions

StatusPrototype

8.1 Purpose#

Conditions are the shared gameplay language connecting Spells, Talents, Enemies, Encounters, and party members. They allow simple abilities to interact without requiring every Spell to contain a long, unique ruleset.

Individual Condition definitions and values belong in the Condition Bible.

8.2 Condition Philosophy#

Conditions should be intuitive, readable, and recognizable. Their primary purpose is to create meaningful decisions and interactions rather than accumulate passive numerical complexity.

Conditions should be shared across Color Schools when appropriate. Shared Conditions allow players with different Builds to contribute to the same tactical objective.

8.3 Categories#

Conditions may include:

  • Damage over Time
  • Soft Crowd Control
  • Hard Crowd Control
  • Utility
  • Buffs
  • Debuffs
  • Conditions tagged as Combat Resources

A Condition should have one clearly described primary function even when it carries additional tags.

8.4 Combat Resource Tag#

Momentum and Weakened count as Conditions and carry the special Combat Resource tag.

  • Momentum is held and managed by a character.
  • Weakened is a single stack applied to an Enemy, may be refreshed, and may be consumed by selected effects.

The tag allows Spells and Talents to refer to both systems consistently without pretending that they behave identically.

8.5 Setup and Payoff#

Many Conditions support setup-and-payoff gameplay. One player may apply or maintain a Condition while another Spell, Talent, or party member benefits from it.

These interactions should reward cooperation and planning without creating mandatory pairings. A Build should remain functional even when an ideal payoff does not appear.

8.6 Crowd Control#

Soft Crowd Control limits part of a target's behavior. Hard Crowd Control temporarily prevents meaningful action.

Hard Crowd Control should generally involve setup, shorter duration, diminishing returns, immunity rules, or other readable limitations. Bosses and selected Enemies may resist or ignore specific effects when required by their design.

8.7 Readability#

Icons, visual effects, unit frames, nameplates, animation, and audio should communicate important Conditions without overwhelming the player. The number of simultaneously important Conditions should remain manageable.

Chapter 9 — Talents

StatusPrototype

9.1 Purpose#

Talents glue Builds together. Spells define active capabilities, Equipment provides much of the numerical foundation, and Talents connect mechanics into a more distinct and satisfying whole.

Individual Talent entries, values, acquisition weights, and implementation status belong in the Talent Bible.

9.2 Talent Philosophy#

Talents should create decisions rather than automatic upgrades. They should usually modify behavior, strengthen an interaction, reward a combat style, or connect existing mechanics.

Talents may provide numerical benefits when appropriate, but should rarely exist only as raw damage increases.

9.3 Build Identity#

Talents add another layer to the Spells a player has acquired. They should not routinely replace the Build's active identity, but may meaningfully define how that Build functions.

Two players with similar Spells should be able to develop different identities through their Talent choices.

9.4 Color Talents#

Each Color School has a Talent pool connected to its fantasy and mechanical language. Players use individually owned Relics for Talent progression associated with the Altars of the Five Old Gods.

The exact offering method remains subject to development. Possible structures include Drafts, weighted offerings, or other controlled selection systems.

9.5 Vertical and Horizontal Progression#

Vertical Talents strengthen mechanics within an existing Color identity. Horizontal Talents create interactions between multiple Colors.

Both approaches support replayability. Vertical progression reinforces specialization, while horizontal progression supports creative mixed-Color Builds.

9.6 MVP Development#

Talents belong to the scope of the complete MVP but may be implemented after the first Combat, Spell, and Equipment milestone. This staging protects development focus without removing Talents from the progression foundation being validated.

Chapter 10 — Equipment

StatusPrototype

10.1 Terminology#

Item is the broad category for objects that may be obtained or used. Equipment means Items that can be equipped by a character. Future Consumables may therefore be Items without being Equipment.

This chapter focuses on Equipment. Individual pieces, statistics, effects, and implementation status belong in the Equipment Bible.

10.2 Equipment Philosophy#

Equipment provides much of a character's statistical progression during a run. It strengthens auto attacks, Spells, survivability, resources, and other scalable mechanics without routinely replacing the active identity created by Spells and Talents.

Equipment should support Builds rather than define them through one mandatory drop.

10.3 Character Statistics#

Current gameplay uses direct Character Statistics such as Physical Power, Spell Power, and Healing Power. Equipment also contributes to survival and resource capacity through relevant statistics.

Primary Attributes remain a Future / Under Evaluation possibility and must not be described as part of the current implementation.

10.4 Ownership and Trading#

Equipment is individually held and equipped. In the MVP, players may freely trade Equipment in the preparation area. In the finished game, Equipment should be tradable anywhere outside combat.

The absence of a shared inventory does not make allocation a purely personal decision. Players should discuss which character and Build can use a drop most effectively for the party's survival.

10.5 Random Drops and the Shop#

Random Enemy drops create excitement and encourage adaptation. Poor luck should not make a run unwinnable.

The Shop complements random drops by offering controlled opportunities to purchase Equipment with individually owned Gold. It should reduce frustration without replacing the value of Encounter rewards.

10.6 Equipment Diversity#

Most Equipment should remain easy to understand and should create meaningful statistical priorities. A limited number of pieces may eventually contain distinctive passive effects when those effects support rather than replace Build identity.

Advanced rarity systems, crafting, set bonuses, legendary effects, and active Items remain future possibilities until the core Equipment foundation is validated.

Chapter 11 — Enemy Design

StatusPrototype

11.1 Purpose#

Enemies are the primary challenge throughout a run. Their purpose is not only to threaten players, but to encourage teamwork, target prioritization, positioning, and adaptation.

Every Encounter should ask the party to solve a problem rather than merely reduce a large Health pool. Individual Enemy definitions, abilities, AI parameters, and implementation status belong in the Enemy Bible.

11.2 Simple Enemies. Deep Encounters.#

Most Enemies should have a clear battlefield role and a limited number of understandable abilities. Complexity should emerge primarily from the interaction of multiple Enemies, their positioning, their behavior, the Combat Area, and the party's current capabilities.

This principle does not prohibit complex elite Enemies, Mini Bosses, or Bosses. It establishes clarity as the default and requires additional complexity to justify itself.

11.3 Battlefield Roles#

Enemy roles describe a unit's purpose in combat rather than its visual identity, faction, or species. Typical roles may include:

  • Brawler
  • Tank
  • Assassin
  • Ranger
  • Caster
  • Healer
  • Support
  • Summoner
  • Specialist

An Enemy may combine roles, but players should still be able to understand its most important purpose.

11.4 Modular AI#

Enemy behavior should be modular and data-driven. A unit is primarily defined by its role, positioning behavior, target priorities, triggers, abilities, and reactions.

This structure should allow the team to create new Enemies by recombining proven behaviors and abilities rather than requiring entirely unique AI for every unit. Modularity should support variety without making different Enemies feel interchangeable.

11.5 Tactical Purpose#

Enemies should create decisions involving:

  • Positioning
  • Target priority
  • Interrupts
  • Crowd Control
  • Threat management
  • Protection of allies
  • Movement and spacing
  • Resource and cooldown timing

Enemy combinations should encourage players to coordinate rather than fight as isolated individuals.

11.6 Threat and Targeting#

Normal Enemy targeting should interact predictably with Threat. A Tank who uses the available tools correctly should be able to influence Enemy attention reliably.

Selected Enemies may ignore or override normal Threat through explicit abilities and Encounter rules. These exceptions should be readable and purposeful, such as an Assassin seeking a vulnerable backline target. Hidden or arbitrary targeting should be avoided.

11.7 Readability and Counterplay#

Players should understand what an Enemy is attempting to do. Silhouette, animation, effects, sound, movement, and behavior should communicate the unit's battlefield role and immediate intention.

Telegraphs should be used where they improve understanding, but not every attack requires the same presentation. Players should learn by paying attention to consistent visual and behavioral language.

Every dangerous Enemy behavior should offer appropriate counterplay through positioning, timing, target priority, control, mitigation, or another understandable response.

11.8 Difficulty and Scaling#

Enemy difficulty may increase through:

  • Additional or improved abilities
  • More intelligent behavior
  • Stronger Enemy combinations
  • Increased statistical strength
  • Encounter-specific mechanics
  • Elite variants
  • Higher execution requirements

Mechanics and higher statistics work together. Numerical scaling makes increasing power readable and allows units to remain relevant at different points in a run, while behavioral and mechanical changes prevent difficulty from becoming purely a Health-and-damage increase.

Adaptive behavior that reacts to player Builds remains a possible future development and must not be assumed as an MVP requirement.

12

Chapter 12 — Encounter Design

StatusPrototype

12.1 Purpose#

Encounters are where the game's systems meet. They combine Enemy Groups with a Combat Area and its environmental challenges to create tactical problems requiring communication and deliberate decisions.

12.2 Canonical Encounter Definition#

An Encounter is a label used by the development team for one or more Enemy Groups designed as a tactical challenge within a Section. It does not create a hard gameplay boundary.

An Enemy Group is a set of units designed to engage as a connected group. A Combat Area is the environment in which Enemy Groups are placed and includes its environmental challenges.

Where the level permits, players may pull additional Enemy Groups into an active fight, combine groups from different designed Encounters, disengage, or skip an Encounter. These choices may increase risk or forfeit attached rewards such as Experience, Gold, and Equipment. Players remain free to choose how they move through and approach a Section.

12.3 Encounter Philosophy#

Simple Enemies should create deep Encounters. Difficulty should emerge from Enemy combinations, positioning, behavior, and the Combat Area with its environmental challenges rather than relying only on individually complicated units.

Each Encounter should have a recognizable identity and ask the party to solve a particular tactical problem.

12.4 Teaching Through Gameplay#

New mechanics should be introduced gradually and communicated through readable behavior. Early Encounters should provide room to observe, react, and recover from mistakes. Later Encounters may combine familiar ideas and demand more precise execution.

Failure is part of learning, but frustration caused by hidden rules should be avoided. Encounters should teach through consistent presentation and consequences instead of relying entirely on external tutorials.

12.5 Composition#

Enemy roles should complement or complicate one another in understandable ways. A Tank may protect a Healer, a Support may strengthen a Brawler, or an Assassin may pressure an exposed backline.

Not every Encounter requires every role. Composition should serve the Encounter's purpose rather than follow a fixed checklist.

Environmental challenges, patrols, positioning, and the shape of the Combat Area may be as important as the Enemy list.

12.6 Tactical Decision-Making#

Good Encounters create decisions about:

  • Target priority
  • Positioning
  • Pull and engagement timing
  • Interrupts
  • Crowd Control
  • Threat management
  • Protection of allies
  • Use of limited resources and cooldowns
  • Adaptation when the original plan fails

Multiple valid solutions should exist whenever possible so that different party compositions can succeed through different strengths.

12.7 Difficulty Curve#

Difficulty should increase through new mechanics, smarter combinations, higher execution requirements, and statistical scaling. Earlier Encounters teach the shared language of the game. Later Encounters recombine and test that knowledge.

Encounter pacing should alternate pressure with opportunities to understand rewards, discuss strategy, and prepare for what comes next.

12.8 Variety and Replayability#

Variety may come from Enemy combinations, Combat Area layouts, environmental challenges, objectives, modifiers, and the party's current Builds.

Variation should remain readable. Randomness should not combine elements without regard for counterplay or produce challenges that the party could not reasonably understand.

12.9 Boss Preparation#

Regular Encounters should teach concepts that may later reappear in Mini Boss and Boss fights. Major Encounters can combine, transform, or expand those ideas.

Bosses may also introduce unique mechanics, but completely unfamiliar rules should be used carefully and communicated clearly.

13

Chapter 13 — Boss Design

StatusPrototype

13.1 Purpose#

Mini Bosses and Bosses are memorable milestones that test the party's understanding of Combat, cooperation, and Build development.

They should act as meaningful tests of unfocused play and weak party coordination without requiring the exact memorization and execution associated with a highly punishing action game.

13.2 Boss Philosophy#

A Boss fight should feel like solving a demanding Encounter, not merely defeating an oversized Enemy with a large Health pool.

Boss difficulty comes from both:

  • Mechanics, behavior, phases, supporting units, and environmental interaction.
  • Higher statistics appropriate to the milestone and the party's progression.

Mechanics remain the primary source of identity, while statistical strength reinforces the importance of the challenge.

13.3 Learning and Mastery#

Bosses may test mechanics introduced in earlier Encounters, combine familiar concepts, and introduce selected unique ideas.

Unorthodox mechanics should be used sparingly and communicated clearly. Players should understand why they failed and what they could attempt differently.

13.4 Encounter Structure#

A Boss Encounter should have a readable flow. Phases, changing priorities, additional Enemies, and environmental challenges may keep the fight dynamic without making it chaotic.

Every phase should have a purpose and should remain connected to the Boss's fantasy and the skills being tested.

13.5 Teamwork#

Bosses should create reasons to communicate about positioning, targets, interrupts, Crowd Control, defensive actions, cooldowns, Threat, and party roles.

Different party compositions should be able to solve the Encounter through different strengths whenever possible.

13.6 Readability#

Animation, visual effects, audio, movement, and battlefield changes should communicate important Boss mechanics. Players should be able to connect success and failure to visible events rather than hidden rules.

13.7 MVP Milestones#

In the MVP:

  • Encounter 5 contains a Mini Boss.
  • Encounter 10 contains a Mini Boss.
  • Encounter 15 contains a Mini Boss.
  • Encounter 20 contains the End Boss.

13.8 Boss Identity#

Every Boss should have a recognizable fantasy and memorable mechanics. Players should remember how the party solved the fight, not only how much Health the Boss possessed.

Individual Boss specifications belong in the Enemy Bible or a future dedicated Boss Bible if their number and complexity justify a separate document.

14

Chapter 14 — Level Design

StatusEarly Concept

14.1 Purpose#

Level Design provides the stage on which Combat, exploration, progression, and decision-making take place. Levels should actively support gameplay rather than function only as visual environments.

14.2 Level Philosophy#

The Dream Dungeon should feel like an adventure rather than a sequence of disconnected arenas. Its structure should encourage exploration, risk-versus-reward decisions, and meaningful route planning.

Gameplay readability takes priority over visual complexity. Environments should reinforce atmosphere and identity without hiding important navigation or combat information.

14.3 Final Dungeon Structure#

The final journey contains six Acts. Each Act contains one Dungeon. Each Dungeon contains multiple branching Sections.

Sections may be:

  • Neutral
  • Influenced by one or more Color Schools
  • Focused on Points of Interest
  • Structured around Boss or milestone progression
  • Built around one or more Encounters

Routes may use blocked paths and points of no return so that players cannot clear every possible branch. This makes route selection part of long-term strategy.

14.4 Combat Areas and Encounters#

Sections may contain multiple Combat Areas and designed Encounters. An Encounter describes an intended grouping of Enemy Groups rather than a hard boundary tied to one Combat Area. Where the layout permits, players may move between Combat Areas, combine Enemy Groups from different designed Encounters, or skip them.

Combat Areas and their environmental challenges should be designed around the Enemy Groups placed within them. Open rooms, narrow corridors, bridges, caverns, courtyards, natural clearings, and Boss chambers may create different tactical situations without requiring a different combat system.

14.5 Tactical Spaces#

Combat Areas may use line of sight, choke points, elevation, hazards, and available space to influence tactics. The environment should provide meaningful decisions without overshadowing Enemy behavior or producing unreadable obstruction.

14.6 Points of Interest#

Points of Interest provide opportunities outside standard Encounters. Possibilities include Shops, Wells, Altars, treasure locations, challenges, events, lore locations, and future story interactions.

Points of Interest should create decisions rather than simply grant free rewards. Their presence, cost, route position, or risk should matter.

14.7 Biomes and Color Influence#

Biomes and Color influence should communicate identity through architecture, vegetation, Enemies, music, environmental storytelling, and mechanics.

A Dungeon may contain multiple stretches or Sections with different Color influences. These transitions should remain coherent within the larger Act and Dungeon.

14.8 The MVP Level#

The MVP replaces the final branching structure with one Combat Arena and a connected MVP-only Central Hub. Both terms describe the MVP-only prototype structure.

The Combat Arena provides a controlled environment for testing Combat, Enemy behavior, Encounters, Bosses, and progression. The MVP-only Central Hub provides a calm preparation space between Encounters.

The final game replaces this structure with branching Dungeons and Sections. The MVP structure must not be mistaken for the long-term level vision.

14.9 Future Development#

Procedural generation, hidden routes, biome-specific mechanics, environmental storytelling, puzzles, dynamic events, and expanded Points of Interest remain future development areas.

Their implementation should begin only after the core gameplay foundation is sufficiently validated.

15

Chapter 15 — Setting

StatusEarly Concept

15.1 Purpose#

The setting gives context to the player's journey and connects Combat, exploration, Enemies, Bosses, progression, and narrative into one coherent world.

DreamDungeon should primarily feel like a grounded dark-fantasy dungeon adventure. The hidden dream reality explains the world but should not turn its moment-to-moment presentation into an abstract or constantly surreal dreamscape.

15.2 Player Experience#

Players experience the Dream Dungeon as an ancient, dangerous, mysterious, and ever-changing place beneath the physical world. Every run feels like another descent into its depths.

The Dungeon should evoke:

  • Mystery
  • Danger
  • Wonder
  • Discovery
  • Decay
  • Corruption
  • Forgotten history
  • The presence of something ancient and alive

Visceral imagery and gore may be used where they strengthen a specific Enemy, biome, mechanic, or narrative moment. Gore is not a mandatory central tone pillar and should not reduce readability or become a substitute for atmosphere.

15.3 World Belief#

Most inhabitants believe the Dream Dungeon is a physical place connected to the conflict between the Five Old Gods and a hostile divine being called the New God.

They believe the Five Old Gods represent the established divine order and that the New God corrupts creation and threatens the world. Religions, myths, scholarship, and NPC language reflect this belief to varying degrees.

The changing layout of the Dungeon is accepted as one of its many mysteries. People may disagree about its origin, purpose, and history.

15.4 Player Knowledge#

At the beginning of the game, the player shares the world's common assumptions. The player believes the characters physically descend into the Dream Dungeon and does not know that they have entered a dreamwalking trance.

Dreamlike inconsistencies should appear as subtle clues rather than immediate explanations. Altered layouts, familiar places appearing differently, unexplained recognition, and fragments of memory may become meaningful only in hindsight.

15.5 Objective Truth#

The Dream Dungeon is the subconscious mind of the Ancient One.

The Five Old Gods place chosen mortals into a dreamwalking trance, allowing them to enter that subconscious without understanding what has happened. The Dungeon's changing structure reflects memories, fears, emotions, and the reorganization of thought rather than conventional physical architecture.

The Ancient One created the Five Old Gods. The Old Gods later imprisoned or subdued the Ancient One and shaped the world's account of that conflict.

Despite this truth, the Dungeon should remain believable as a dark-fantasy place. The dream origin should explain its impossible behavior without dominating every visual or gameplay decision.

15.6 Biomes and Environmental Storytelling#

Biomes communicate identity through architecture, vegetation, Enemy selection, Color influence, music, environmental mechanics, and world details.

History should be discovered through ruins, objects, shrines, remains, changing environments, optional interactions, and other forms of environmental storytelling. Exposition should not interrupt the core gameplay loop unnecessarily.

15.7 MVP Boundary#

The MVP contains minimal explicit narrative. Placeholder representations, environmental hints, and enough context to support gameplay are sufficient while Combat, Encounters, and progression are being validated.

16

Chapter 16 — Lore

StatusEarly Concept

16.1 Purpose#

Lore gives meaning and context to the journey, strengthens immersion, and rewards curiosity. It should never be required to understand core gameplay.

DreamDungeon is not primarily story-driven. Mystery is often more powerful than complete explanation, and historical accounts may be incomplete, biased, or deliberately false.

16.2 Objective Creation History#

The Ancient One created the Five Old Gods. The Old Gods later turned against, imprisoned, or otherwise subdued their creator.

The exact circumstances, motivations, and method remain areas for future narrative development. The relationship itself is canonical; the complete historical sequence is not yet finalized.

The Dream Dungeon is the Ancient One's subconscious rather than a conventional physical prison. Chosen Dream Walkers enter it through a trance created by the Old Gods.

16.3 World History and Belief#

The modern world generally remembers the Five Old Gods as ancient divine powers and the New God as a corrupt enemy that threatens their order.

Historical records have been lost, altered, misunderstood, or deliberately shaped. Scholars and religions may disagree, but most remain inside the false framework established by the Old Gods.

Rumors about the Dungeon containing the essence, weakness, or means to defeat the New God may exist within the world. Such statements represent belief or propaganda, not automatically Objective Truth.

16.4 Ancient One and New God#

Ancient One is used for Objective Truth, internal design documentation, and ancient history.

New God is used for modern religion, myth, NPC language, World Belief, and the player's early understanding.

These terms do not describe two separate beings. They describe one being through different historical and ideological perspectives.

16.5 History Through Discovery#

Players should uncover lore through exploration, environmental storytelling, optional interactions, Enemy design, Bosses, relics, journals, NPCs, and world events.

Important discoveries should feel earned. The game may preserve ambiguity where several interpretations strengthen discussion without making the core plot incoherent.

16.6 Guardians#

Dungeon guardians use a mixed origin. They may be manifestations of the Ancient One's memories or fears, subconscious defenses, or beings and entities bound within that subconscious.

To the player, they should appear as grounded dark-fantasy monsters, cultists, beasts, guardians, and factions. Their dream origin should become a deeper explanation rather than an excuse for arbitrary design.

16.7 MVP Boundary#

The MVP requires little explicit lore. Environmental hints and placeholder descriptions are sufficient until gameplay systems have been validated.

17

Chapter 17 — The Five Old Gods

StatusEarly Concept

17.1 Purpose#

The Five Old Gods provide thematic foundations for the Color Schools, Biomes, Altars, Relics, Talents, Spells, and world identity.

They should feel ancient, powerful, and mysterious. Their influence is present throughout gameplay rather than limited to dialogue or quest-giving.

17.2 Objective Truth#

The Ancient One created the Five Old Gods. The Old Gods later imprisoned or subdued their creator.

They knowingly place Dream Walkers into a trance and guide them into the Ancient One's subconscious. They conceal this truth and present the Ancient One as the hostile New God.

The Old Gods grant Dream Walkers access to their powers through Spells during these expeditions.

17.3 World Belief#

The world believes the Five Old Gods represent the established divine order and that the New God is a hostile, corrupting power.

Religions, myths, symbols, landscapes, and magical traditions reinforce this narrative. The exact public interpretation may vary by culture, region, and follower.

17.4 Player Knowledge#

Early in the game, the player accepts the Old Gods' account and understands their power as necessary help against the New God and the Dungeon's guardians.

The player should not initially know that the Old Gods created the dreamwalking trance, that the Dungeon is the Ancient One's subconscious, or that the New God is the Ancient One seen through a false historical perspective.

17.5 Gameplay Connections#

The Old Gods connect directly to:

  • Color Schools
  • Spells
  • Biomes
  • Altars
  • Relics
  • Talents
  • Visual and audio identity
  • World history and narrative perspective

These connections should reinforce gameplay. Lore should deepen systems the player already experiences rather than exist as a separate layer with no mechanical expression.

17.6 Individual Identities#

Each Old God should eventually have a distinct personality, philosophy, visual language, domain, symbols, followers, and relationship to the others.

The current Early Concept working identities are:

  • Red God — God of Rage
  • Blue God — God of Delusion
  • Green God — God of Cunning
  • Black God — God of End
  • White God — God of Mercy

Those identities should influence Spells, Enemies, Bosses, environments, and rewards without forcing each Color into one party role.

The detailed identities and histories remain Early Concept material and belong to future worldbuilding work.

17.7 MVP Boundary#

The MVP requires only enough representation of the Old Gods to support Color identity, Spell acquisition, Relics, and the staged Talent system. Full narrative delivery is postponed.

18

Chapter 18 — Story

StatusEarly Concept

18.1 Purpose#

The story gives context to repeated descents, provides long-term motivation, and gradually changes the player's understanding of the world.

Gameplay remains the priority. Narrative should reward interested players without repeatedly interrupting Combat, exploration, and Build creation.

18.2 Objective Truth#

Chosen mortals are unknowingly placed into a dreamwalking trance by the Five Old Gods. Their expeditions take place within the subconscious of the Ancient One.

The Old Gods conceal the truth and frame the Ancient One as the New God, a hostile divine enemy. The Ancient One eventually reveals the real nature of the Dream Dungeon and challenges the history the player has accepted.

18.3 World Belief#

The world believes that chosen adventurers descend into a physical Dungeon to oppose the New God and protect or restore the order of the Five Old Gods.

This belief supplies the early motivation for the characters and provides a coherent surface narrative before the deeper truth is discovered.

18.4 Player Knowledge and Reveal Structure#

The player initially understands the world through its common beliefs. No early chapter, tutorial, dialogue, or presentation should imply that the characters knowingly enter a dream.

Clues should accumulate gradually through environments, repeated patterns, altered memories, Enemy design, relics, Bosses, and optional discoveries.

The Ancient One's revelation should recontextualize earlier experiences rather than introduce a disconnected twist.

18.5 Recurring Dream Walkers#

The same Dream Walkers return across failed runs without consciously understanding that continuity. Mechanically and subjectively, every run feels like a fresh character and begins without direct power from the previous attempt.

The exact mechanism—awakening, revival, memory suppression, or another form of return—remains an Open Question for later story development.

This structure should preserve both the emotional loss of a developed run-specific character and the deeper narrative idea that the same people repeatedly enter the Ancient One's subconscious.

18.6 Narrative Delivery#

Story should primarily be delivered through:

  • Environmental storytelling
  • Optional dialogue
  • NPCs
  • Journals and records
  • Relics and Altars
  • Enemy and Boss identity
  • World events
  • Memories and visions
  • Changes that become meaningful across repeated runs

Cutscenes are not currently planned as a primary delivery method. Any future cinematic presentation must support rather than dominate the gameplay rhythm.

18.7 Player Agency and Final Conflict#

Players should feel like participants rather than spectators. Narrative discoveries may affect how the party understands the Old Gods, the Ancient One, and the purpose of the expedition.

A final choice between the Old Gods and the Ancient One remains a Future Idea. It is not yet canonical and should not constrain the story foundation prematurely.

18.8 MVP Boundary#

The MVP contains only a basic premise, placeholder narrative elements, and enough thematic context to support gameplay. The dreamwalking revelation and complete story structure are not required for early gameplay validation.

Chapter 19 — MVP Philosophy

StatusPrototype

19.1 Purpose#

The Minimum Viable Product exists to answer one central question:

Is DreamDungeon's core gameplay compelling enough to justify building the complete game?

Every MVP feature should contribute directly to validating the Core Gameplay Loop, cooperative play, Build development, or the technical foundation required to test them.

19.2 MVP Philosophy#

The MVP prioritizes gameplay quality over content quantity. Placeholder graphics, visual effects, sound, music, menus, and interfaces are acceptable when they communicate gameplay sufficiently for meaningful testing.

The MVP is a gameplay prototype rather than a demonstration of final content volume or production quality.

19.3 Core Validation Goals#

The MVP should test whether:

  • Combat feels responsive, impactful, and satisfying.
  • Cooperation encourages communication and tactical decision-making.
  • Encounters are readable and engaging.
  • Spell Drafts create meaningful individual and party choices.
  • Equipment creates satisfying power growth and cooperative trading decisions.
  • Talents eventually strengthen Build identity.
  • Different Builds feel meaningfully different.
  • Runs remain engaging over their intended duration.
  • Bosses and Mini Bosses create memorable milestones.
  • Multiplayer, Enemy AI, saving, and longer sessions provide a stable technical foundation.

19.4 MVP Structure#

The MVP uses one Combat Arena and one connected MVP-only Central Hub. Both are explicitly MVP-only structures.

The run contains 20 Encounters:

  • Encounters 5, 10, and 15 contain Mini Bosses.
  • Encounter 20 contains the End Boss.

A party defeat ends the run. A successful run ends after the End Boss is defeated.

19.5 Included Systems#

The complete MVP scope includes:

  • Character movement and core controls
  • Multiplayer
  • Combat
  • Spells and shared Spell Drafts
  • Health, Mana, Momentum, Weakened, Threat, Experience, Gold, and Relics as applicable
  • Conditions
  • Equipment
  • Talents through staged implementation
  • Enemy AI
  • Encounters
  • Mini Bosses and the End Boss
  • Saving and resuming across sessions
  • Essential menus and user interface
  • Sufficient placeholder art, visual effects, sound, and music to communicate gameplay

Talents may arrive after the first playable Combat, Spell, and Equipment milestone while remaining part of the complete MVP.

19.6 Player Count#

Current testing and balance target a four-player party.

The final supported range remains open. Possibilities include one to five players, explicit scaling for smaller parties such as two or three players, and a future party finder. These possibilities must be validated rather than presented as commitments.

19.7 Color Scope#

The exact number and selection of MVP Color Schools remain open. The MVP should implement the minimum set needed to validate Color identity, Spell Drafting, Build diversity, and party interaction.

19.8 Excluded or Postponed Systems#

The MVP postpones:

  • The complete six-Act Dungeon
  • Full procedural generation and branching exploration
  • Expanded Points of Interest
  • Additional Color Schools beyond the validated MVP set
  • Spell Ranks and Spell Evolution
  • Advanced Talents beyond the required validation scope
  • Advanced itemization, crafting, and set systems
  • Complete story and lore delivery
  • Cinematics and voice acting
  • Endless Mode
  • Advanced graphics and accessibility systems beyond the essential foundation

Postponed systems may remain important to the final vision. They are excluded because they are not currently necessary to answer the MVP's central question.

19.9 Playtesting Philosophy#

Every prototype and playtest should begin with specific questions. Feedback should focus on enjoyment, readability, cooperation, pacing, Build variety, and technical reliability.

Features may be changed or removed when testing shows that they do not improve the experience. Previous effort does not justify preserving a feature that conflicts with the game's principles.

19.10 Success Criteria#

The MVP succeeds when players consistently report and demonstrate that:

  • Combat is enjoyable.
  • Cooperation feels rewarding.
  • Builds feel distinct.
  • Drafts and rewards create meaningful decisions.
  • Encounters remain understandable and tactically engaging.
  • Boss milestones feel memorable.
  • Players want to begin another run after success or failure.

Chapter 20 — Development Roadmap Philosophy

StatusLiving

20.1 Purpose#

DreamDungeon should be developed from the inside out. The quality of the Core Gameplay Loop is more important than the quantity of content.

Every development stage should build on a foundation that has been implemented, tested, and understood rather than assuming that earlier systems already work.

20.2 Development Order#

The general development order is:

  1. Validate Combat, Enemy behavior, Encounters, Spell Drafting, Equipment, multiplayer, and the pacing of a complete MVP run.
  2. Complete and deepen Talents, Spells, Conditions, Equipment, Enemies, and Bosses.
  3. Expand into branching Sections, Dungeons, procedural generation, Points of Interest, Biomes, and environmental storytelling.
  4. Increase content variety while respecting the proven system foundations.
  5. Polish balance, readability, visuals, audio, accessibility, performance, and overall user experience.

Playtesting accompanies every stage.

20.3 Living Roadmap#

Exact milestones and current priorities belong in the separate Roadmap. They should not be duplicated in this Bible because production order will change more frequently than the underlying development philosophy.

Chapter 21 — Open Questions & Decision Discipline

StatusLiving

21.1 Purpose#

Open Questions identify areas that require discussion, prototyping, technical investigation, or playtesting. An unresolved question is not a documentation failure when its uncertainty is explicit.

21.2 Decision Philosophy#

Whenever possible, gameplay should answer design questions that theory alone cannot resolve. The team should distinguish among:

  • Questions that require a design decision before implementation.
  • Variables that can remain configurable during prototyping.
  • Questions that require technical investigation.
  • Questions that should be answered through playtesting.
  • Future possibilities that do not yet require an answer.

21.3 Decision Workflow#

When an Open Question is resolved:

  1. Record the accepted decision and reasoning.
  2. Update the canonical Philosophy Bible, Technical GDD, System Bible, or Glossary entry.
  3. Update dependent information through cross-references rather than copied explanations.
  4. Record meaningful changes in the Changelog.
  5. Mark the former question as resolved rather than silently deleting its history.

21.4 Living Register#

Specific questions, owners, status, validation methods, and outcomes belong in the separate Open Questions and Decisions register.

Current examples include final player count, MVP Color selection, the exact return mechanism of recurring Dream Walkers, balance values, procedural generation, and detailed story history.

Chapter 22 — Future Ideas & Scope Discipline

StatusLiving

22.1 Purpose#

Future Ideas provide a safe place for creativity without turning every interesting possibility into a production commitment.

Recording an idea does not approve it for implementation.

22.2 Evaluation Principles#

Before a Future Idea enters active development, the team should ask:

  • Does it reinforce the Core Experience?
  • Does it solve a meaningful player or production problem?
  • Does it create interesting decisions?
  • Can existing systems express the idea more clearly?
  • Is the current foundation ready for the added complexity?
  • What must be removed, delayed, or maintained if this idea is added?

22.3 Idea Areas#

Future areas may include:

  • Alternative Spell Draft structures
  • Spell Evolution and Ranks
  • Advanced Talents
  • Active Items and Consumables
  • Additional Condition interactions
  • Crafting and gatherable resources
  • Expanded Points of Interest and Dungeon events
  • Environmental puzzles and hidden routes
  • Additional Enemy families and elite modifiers
  • Adaptive Enemy behavior
  • Alternative Encounter objectives
  • Expanded NPCs, factions, and narrative events
  • Endless Mode and challenge modifiers
  • Mod and add-on support
  • Advanced accessibility and interface customization
  • Additional multiplayer features and party finding

The separate Future Ideas register is the canonical home for individual concepts, their status, and their evaluation history.

  • Dungeon — The primary level structure within one Act.
  • Section — A branching subdivision of a Dungeon. Tier is not used for this concept.
  • Combat Area — The environment in which Enemy Groups are placed, including its environmental challenges. It is not a hard Encounter boundary.
  • Combat Arena — The MVP-only level used to prototype the sequence of Combat Encounters.
  • Central Hub — The MVP-only preparation area connected to the Combat Arena.
  • Enemy Group — A set of Enemies designed to engage as a connected group.
  • Encounter — A label used by the development team for one or more Enemy Groups designed as a tactical challenge. It does not create a hard gameplay boundary.
  • Mini Boss — The milestone challenge at MVP Encounters 5, 10, and 15.
  • End Boss — The final challenge at MVP Encounter 20.
  • Spell — A primary active player capability acquired through the Spell system.
  • Build — The temporary combination of Spells, Equipment, Talents, Conditions, and related interactions defining a character during a run.
  • Item — The broad category for obtainable or usable objects.
  • Equipment — Equippable Items that provide Character Statistics and related effects.
  • Condition — A named gameplay state with defined behavior and interaction rules.
  • Combat Resource — A special tag used by Momentum and Weakened while both remain Conditions.
  • Momentum — A character-held Condition tagged as a Combat Resource.
  • Weakened — A single stack-based Enemy Condition tagged as a Combat Resource; it may be refreshed or consumed by defined effects.
  • Threat — The combat value influencing which player an Enemy prefers to attack.
  • Color School — One of five thematic and mechanical identities associated with the Five Old Gods.
  • Tank — The approved role term. Frontliner is not used as its replacement.
  • Ancient One — The objective historical identity used in internal documentation and ancient truth.
  • New God — The modern world's belief and the player's early understanding of the Ancient One.
  • Dream Walker — A chosen mortal unknowingly placed into a dreamwalking trance by the Old Gods.

Closing Principle

DreamDungeon should evolve through implementation and playtesting without losing the identity that makes it worth building.

New systems, content, and production decisions should strengthen the central experience of overcoming dangerous Encounters through teamwork, planning, and creative Builds. When a feature adds complexity without reinforcing that experience, the team should be willing to reconsider it.

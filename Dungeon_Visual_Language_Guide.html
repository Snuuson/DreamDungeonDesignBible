Ability readability across every act

One global ability system

Green, Black, White, Red, and Blue abilities keep the same identity throughout the entire dungeon. The active act changes the environment—not the spell's palette.

Always use a contrast pair

Build every effect with at least two values from its ability group: a dark core or outline plus a bright glow, edge, particle, or impact flash. Never use only a middle value.

Adapt to the current light

In Acts 1–3, lead with the dark core or outline against bright scenery. In Acts 4–6, lead with the glow or flash against dark scenery. Preserve the same ability hue in both cases.

Test the difficult matches

Check Green against grass and cave plants, Blue against cave haze, Red against fire, and Black against the deepest dark. The spell must still read in grayscale and at a quick glance.

Game design guide

1. Start with the act

Choose the current act's six colors first. They define the lighting, materials, sky, terrain, clothing, and atmosphere for that part of the game.

2. Build scenes in layers

Use a dark or muted tone for most of the screen, a supporting midtone for forms and materials, then reserve the brightest or most saturated color for attention.

3. Reserve accents for meaning

  • Gold: interactable or objective
  • Teal: magic or rare reward
  • Lime: poison or corruption
  • Vermilion: immediate danger
  • Violet: elite or otherworldly

4. Make gameplay readable

Never use a gameplay accent as a common background color. Give the player, enemy attacks, interactables, and hazards a distinct value as well as a distinct hue.

5. UI and text

Use Morning Cream or Aged Bone for important light text on Crystalline Void, Stone Dark, or True Pitch. Use color to reinforce icons and labels—not as the only way to communicate state.

6. Check every scene

  • View it in grayscale: does the player still read first?
  • At a glance, can you spot the next objective?
  • Are accent colors used only where they carry meaning?
  • Does the scene still feel like its assigned act?

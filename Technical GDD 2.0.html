Chapter 1 — Game Overview

1.1 Overview#

DreamDungeon is a cooperative fantasy roguelike RPG balanced around a party of four; the final supported player range remains under evaluation. Each run resets its power progression and develops through Spell Drafts, Equipment, and Talents. The game condenses the feeling of leveling an MMORPG character into a replayable cooperative adventure without relying on permanent power growth.

This Technical Game Design Document defines DreamDungeon's current design target for the intended final game. The Game Philosophy Bible remains authoritative for design philosophy; this document specifies how the game's systems are intended to work and identifies reduced MVP differences where relevant.

1.2.2 Core Gameplay Pillars#

The current design is guided by the following gameplay pillars. The Game Philosophy Bible contains their authoritative full explanation:

  • Responsive and satisfying real-time combat.
  • Meaningful cooperative gameplay through communication and tactical decision-making.
  • Build creation through Spell Drafts, Equipment and future Talents.
  • High replayability through procedural Build development rather than permanent progression.

1.2.3 Run Structure#

Every run starts from a clean slate. Players gradually develop their characters during the run and lose all progression when the party is defeated. The focus is on replayability rather than long-term character persistence.

1.2.5 Design Constraints#

The following constraints guide current development and help maintain focus during the MVP phase:

  • Gameplay systems are prioritized over content production.
  • Systems are implemented iteratively and refined through playtesting.
  • Placeholder assets are acceptable during prototyping.
  • Features may change or be removed if testing shows they do not improve the game.
  • The MVP is a gameplay prototype rather than a content demonstration.

1.4 Future Expansion#

The following systems are part of the intended final-game Current Design but are excluded from the MVP:

  • Authored procedural Dungeon assembly.
  • Branching paths and Points of Interest.
  • Biomes and Color-influenced Sections.
  • The complete Talent system.
  • Advanced Equipment and Item content.
  • Background storytelling and worldbuilding.

Spell Evolutions, Spell Ranks, crafting, gathering, and Endless Mode remain Future / Under Evaluation rather than committed MVP requirements.

Chapter 2 — Core Gameplay Loop

2.1 Overview#

The Core Gameplay Loop defines a complete final-game run from entry through the final Boss. A smaller MVP loop follows as an explicit validation variant rather than the default design.

2.2 Current Design#

2.2.1 Final Gameplay Loop#

In the final game, the party begins each run with reset power and descends through six Acts of branching Dungeons and authored Sections. Players complete Encounters, gain Experience, acquire Equipment, participate in Spell Drafts, and develop their Builds. The run ends when the party defeats the final Boss or suffers a full party wipe.

StepDescription
Start RunBegin with reset run power while persistent identity remains separate.
Choose RouteNavigate branching paths through authored Sections.
Enter Points of InterestUse Shops, Wells, Altars, treasures, or challenge locations when available.
Complete EncountersDefeat Enemies and earn rewards.
Character ProgressionGain shared XP, Gold, Equipment and Relics.
Boss EncountersMajor milestones throughout the run.
Dungeon BossComplete the current Act and unlock the next Act.
Act ProgressionProgress through a total of six Acts.
Final BossDefeat the final Boss of Act VI and win the game.
Party DefeatRun ends.

2.2.2 MVP Gameplay Loop#

The MVP replaces dungeon exploration with a handcrafted combat arena connected to a central hub to validate combat, Encounters and progression before implementing procedural generation.

FeatureCurrent Design

EncounterOne Encounter representing one dungeon room.
Boss FrequencyAfter Encounters 5, 10, 15 and 20 (End Boss).
ShopPurchase fixed items using Gold.

2.2.3 Encounter Flow#

  • Enter Encounter.
  • Defeat all Enemies.
  • Receive Experience, Gold and Equipment.
  • Return to the Hub.
  • Manage Equipment.
  • Trade with other players
  • Spend Gold.
  • Participate in a Spell Draft when the party levels up.
  • Prepare for the next Encounter.
  • All players Ready.

2.2.4 Victory & Defeat#

ConditionResult
Full party defeatThe run ends.
Final Boss defeatedThe run is completed.
MVP player deathThe player stops participating in the current Encounter. If at least one party member completes it, the dead player is resurrected at the Well afterward.

MVP Gold penaltyA dead player receives no Gold from that Encounter.
Final-game revivalOutside combat, a living player may revive a dead party member through a five-second channeled context interaction. Range, interruption, and multiplayer edge cases remain open.

2.2.5 Session Structure#

A single run is intended to last several hours and may be continued across multiple play sessions. The gameplay loop aims to condense the progression of an MMORPG character into one replayable adventure.

2.3 Open Variables#

  • Spell Draft and leveling cadence.
  • Gold and Shop pacing.
  • Encounter pacing and length.
  • Save ownership, host behavior, failure handling, serialization, and other save-and-resume implementation details.
  • Final-game revival range, interruption, targeting, and multiplayer edge cases.

2.4 Future Expansion#

Branching Dungeon paths, Points of Interest, Biomes, and Color Schools belong to the intended final-game Current Design and are excluded only from the MVP. Random events and other uncommitted loop variations remain Future / Under Evaluation.

Chapter 3 — Combat Systems

3.1 Overview#

Combat is DreamDungeon's central moment-to-moment system. It combines readable tab targeting with telegraphed ground-targeted and directional Spells, so aiming and positioning matter without turning the game into a rapid hack-and-slash experience. The intended rhythm is deliberate tactical action with clear rules, cooperative roles, and configurable values recorded under Open Variables.

3.2 Current Design#

3.2.2 Character Movement#

Players move freely during combat. Positioning is an important part of gameplay and directly influences dodging, threat management and Spell usage.

  • Movement is unrestricted unless affected by gameplay mechanics.
  • Movement speed is modified only through Spells, Conditions or items.
  • Collision and positioning are intended to influence Encounter design.
  • There is phased movement for units, but not for environmental obstacles

3.2.3 Auto Attacks#

Auto attacks provide a reliable baseline source of damage and interact with numerous combat systems. Auto Attacks scale with the weapon damage and with the physical damage stat.

3.2.4 Melee and Ranged Attacks#

Auto Attacks can be melee or ranged, depending on the weapon equipped and the range to the target. Melee combat automatically switches to ranged combat if the target gets out of melee range and vice versa. Ranged auto attacks have a wind up so that you must stand still for a complete attack animation for a successful attack.

3.2.7 Defensive Capabilities#

Players can dodge or block (if shield equipped) Enemy attacks. These chances are increased via gear, stats, Spells or Talents. Block values are also defined by stats provided by items (most commonly the shield itself)

3.2.8 Threat#

Threat determines which player an Enemy prefers to attack. Tank-oriented abilities generate additional Threat via a threat modifier or flat threat increase to allow players to control Enemy focus.

3.2.9 Crowd Control#

  • Soft Crowd Control reduces Enemy effectiveness without removing control completely.
  • Hard Crowd Control temporarily removes Enemy control.
  • Crowd Control Spells may vary in effectiveness depending on the weakened or momentum system
  • Combat resources (momentum, weakened) are defined in Chapter 5 - Resources and the Glossary
  • Conditions and exact definitions are documented in Chapter 8 - Conditions and the Glossary.

3.2.10 Party Roles#

Players are not locked into predefined classes. Roles emerge naturally from Spell Drafts, Equipment and future Talents.

  • Support
  • Controller
  • Hybrid

Chapter 4 — Progression Systems

4.1 Overview#

Progression defines how players become stronger during a run. Character growth is achieved through shared experience, Spell Drafts, Equipment and Talents. All progression is temporary and only lasts for the duration of the current run.

4.2 Current Design#

4.2.1 Progression Flow#

Progression is driven by defeating Encounters. Enemies grant shared experience, allowing the party to level together. Level-ups unlock Spell Drafts. Equipment can be dropped by Enemies at random and can be shared within the party. Gold is dropped by Enemies and split evenly among living party members. It is spent in various ways to improve the character.

SourceReward
EnemiesShared Experience, Gold, Equipment
BossesShared Experience, Gold, Equipment, Relics
Level UpSpell Draft
AltarsTalents
ShopEquipment

4.2.2 Shared Experience#

Experience is shared across the party, so all players level together and no level gap develops within the group. Party levels grant banked Spell Draft opportunities rather than direct Character Statistics.

The current prototype reduces the entire party's Experience reward by 10% for each player who is dead when an Encounter is completed. Dead players remain part of the shared Experience calculation but receive no Gold from that Encounter. Chapter 5 - Resources owns the detailed Experience and Gold rules.

4.2.3 Leveling#

Each level increases the party's overall power by granting access to new Spells through the Spell Draft system. The exact experience curve is subject to balancing.

4.2.4 Spell Drafts#

Party levels bank shared Spell Draft opportunities rather than opening a Draft automatically. The party deliberately starts a Draft outside combat through a collective prayer: a designated hotkey begins a five-second channel that requires the party to gather in close proximity. Completing the channel opens the shared Draft. If several levels are gained before drafting, all earned Drafts remain banked.

  • All players draft simultaneously from the shared selection.
  • Each player chooses one Spell, and each offered Spell can be claimed by only one player.
  • The party may discuss allocation before confirming.
  • A player cannot own duplicate copies of the same Spell.
  • Selected Spells enter the appropriate player's Spell Book for the remainder of the run.
  • Opening a Draft during combat through the same risky channel remains an Open Variable.
  • Disconnects, dead players, interrupted channels, timeouts, forced completion, and multiple banked Drafts require multiplayer edge-case rules.

4.2.5 Spell Book#

All Spells are stored in the Spell Book. Between Encounters players may reorganize their active loadout (Hotbar). Future versions may allow unused Spells to be sacrificed for a partial XP refund.

4.2.6 Equipment#

Equipment increases character statistics and therefore improves auto attacks and Spells that scale with those statistics. Equipment is obtained as loot and may be traded freely between players outside combat.

4.2.7 Relics#

Relics are primarily earned from Boss Encounters. Their main purpose is to unlock Talents at the Altars of the Five Old Gods in the full game.

4.2.8 Talents#

Talents exist to glue Builds together. While Spells define what a player can do and items determine how powerful those actions become, Talents create interactions between Spells, Conditions, colors and playstyles.

4.2.9 Build Growth#

“Character Builds” emerge gradually through the interaction of drafted Spells, equipment and, Talents. Players are encouraged to adapt their Build based on the rewards found during a run.

4.3 Open Variables#

The following values remain under evaluation:

  • Experience curve and level pacing.
  • Spell Draft frequency.
  • Spell Book management and replacement options.
  • Equipment drop rates.
  • Relic acquisition rate.
  • Talent unlock pacing.

4.4 Future Expansion#

  • Spell Ranks through duplicates.
  • Spell Evolutions.
  • Expanded Talent system.
  • Additional progression mechanics: Enchantments, Crafting
  • Meta progression not planned.

Chapter 5 — Resources

5.1 Overview#

Resources are the values players manage throughout a run. They define combat effectiveness, character progression and long-term decision making. Some resources only exist during combat, while others persist for the duration of the run. Together they create the pacing of the game and encourage players to continuously make meaningful decisions throughout an adventure.

5.2 Current Design#

5.2.1 Health#

Health represents a character's survivability. When a player's Health reaches zero, the character dies and can no longer participate in the Encounter until revived or the Encounter ends.

Health is primarily increased through Equipment and can further be influenced by Talents, Conditions and future gameplay systems.

Health is affected by:

  • Enemy attacks and abilities
  • Environmental hazards
  • Certain Spell effects
  • Self-inflicted abilities

Health is restored through:

  • Healing Spells
  • Health regeneration
  • The Well between Encounters
  • Future consumables and effects

Health is one of the most important indicators of Encounter success and directly determines whether the party can continue a run.

5.2.2 Mana#

Mana is the primary resource used to cast Spells. Nearly all Spells consume Mana, making it the limiting factor for sustained Spell usage during combat.

Mana is primarily influenced by Equipment and Mana Regeneration.

Mana is:

  • Consumed when casting Spells
  • Restored through passive Mana Regeneration
  • Restored by the Well between Encounters
  • Potentially restored by Spells, Talents and consumables

Managing Mana efficiently should become an important part of longer Encounters, rewarding players who balance powerful Spell usage with resource conservation.

5.2.3 Momentum#

Momentum is a universal combat resource generated by designated Spells and abilities. It rewards continuous participation in combat and acts as a secondary resource for more advanced gameplay.

Momentum itself does not directly increase player power. Instead, certain Spells scale with current momentum stacks or consume Momentum to gain additional effects such as increased damage, larger areas of effect or stronger crowd control.

Momentum is:

  • Generated by specific Spells and abilities
  • An individually owned stacking Combat Resource.
  • Maximum of 5 stacks
  • Scales designated abilities to have better effects
  • Consumed by designated abilities to have better effects
  • Out of combat one stack is lost every 3 seconds
  • Mostly used by melee abilities to encourage a more active playstyle

5.2.4 Weakened#

Weakened is a universal combat Condition that serves as a setup mechanic for many Spells and abilities.

Unlike traditional debuffs, Weakened has no direct effect on a target by itself. Instead, it enables additional interactions with other Spells. Weakened only has one stack, the duration is reset when reapplied. If a Spell uses the weakened state it is consumed and needs to be reapplied.

Examples include:

  • Increased damage
  • Stronger crowd control
  • Additional secondary effects
  • Spell synergies

This creates meaningful cooperation between different Builds while keeping the number of visible Conditions manageable.

5.2.5 Threat#

Threat determines which player an Enemy prefers to attack.

Every hostile action generates Threat. Some abilities have higher threat modifiers than others. Tanks specialize in manipulating Threat to protect more vulnerable party members.

Threat is generated through:

  • Damage
  • Healing
  • Threat-modifying abilities
  • Future Talents and Equipment

Threat can also be modified by:

  • Flat bonuses
  • Future reduction effects

Threat is one of the core mechanics supporting cooperative gameplay and Encounter design.

5.2.6 Experience#

Experience represents the party's overall progression throughout a run.

Experience is shared between all players, ensuring that the party always progresses together.

Experience is primarily earned from:

  • Defeating Enemies
  • Completing Boss Encounters
  • Future events and quests or hidden treasure

Leveling up grants the party access to a Spell Draft rather than directly increasing character statistics.

Players receive a malus of -10% experience gained for every party member that is dead during an Encounter.

The exact Experience curve remains subject to balancing.

5.2.7 Gold#

Gold is the primary currency used throughout a run.

Rather than providing direct power, Gold allows players to complement random Equipment drops through purchases in the Shop.

Gold is earned from:

  • Enemy kills
  • Boss Encounters
  • Future events and quests or hidden treasure

Gold is:

  • Evenly split between living party members
  • Dead players earn no gold
  • Spent primarily in the Shop

Intended to smooth random loot progression rather than replace it.

The Gold economy remains an important balancing variable throughout development.

5.2.8 Relics#

Relics are rare, individually owned progression rewards obtained primarily from Mini Boss and Boss Encounters. Every eligible player receives their own Relic reward; Relics persist for the run and are spent individually at Altars of the Five Old Gods to begin a Talent Draft.

Relics may be held for a later Altar, making route planning part of long-term Build development. Exact acquisition rate and Talent cost remain balancing variables. Any future conversion into Souls or another resource requires a separate approved design.

5.2.9 Character Statistics#

Character Statistics determine the numerical strength of every character.

Most statistics are primarily increased through Equipment and directly influence combat performance.

Current statistics include:

  • Health
  • Mana
  • Armor
  • Magic Resistance
  • Physical Power
  • Spell Power
  • Healing Power
  • Weapon Critical Strike Chance
  • Attack Speed
  • Mana Regeneration
  • Health Regeneration
  • Block Chance
  • Dodge Chance
  • Future/under evaluation: Spell Haste, Spell Critical Strike Chance, Critical Strike Damage, Parry

Spell Haste is an under-evaluation Character Statistic that would reduce Spell cast times and reduce the interval between periodic ticks for Damage-over-Time effects and Channeled Spells. Spell Critical Strike Chance remains a separate under-evaluation statistic from Weapon Critical Strike Chance.

The exact scaling of individual statistics will continue to evolve during balancing.

5.2.10 Open Variables#

The following values remain under evaluation:

  • Momentum generation rate and per-Spell interactions.
  • Weakened application frequency and duration.
  • Gold economy and Shop pricing.
  • Equipment Statistic scaling.
  • Health and Mana regeneration values.

5.2.11 Future Expansion#

Potential future additions include:

  • Crafting materials
  • Enchanting resources
  • Biome-specific resources
  • Endless mode specific resources

Chapter 6 — Spell System

6.1 Overview#

The Spell System is the primary method through which players interact with combat. Spells define a character's playstyle, determine their role within the party and create the majority of Build diversity throughout a run. Rather than selecting a predefined class, players gradually assemble their own toolkit through Spell Drafts, allowing every character to evolve differently each playthrough.

6.2 Current Design#

6.2.1 Spell Acquisition#

Each player begins a run with one starting Spell obtained through run-start setup or a starter Draft. The exact starter pool and individual records belong in the Spell Bible.

Additional Spells are acquired through the shared Spell Draft progression defined in Chapter 4 - Progression Systems. A selected Spell is added to its owner's Spell Book for the remainder of the run. Chapter 4 - Progression Systems is the canonical source for banking, collective initiation, allocation, and multiplayer Draft rules.

6.2.2 Spell Book#

The Spell Book contains every Spell a player has acquired during the current run.

  • It stores all drafted Spells, including those not currently equipped.
  • In the final game, players may rearrange the active Spell Bar anywhere outside combat.
  • In the MVP, rearrangement is limited to preparation.
  • Spell swapping is disabled during combat.
  • Active and inactive Spells remain available for the run and are lost when the run ends.

Filtering, sorting, and additional management functions remain under evaluation.

6.2.3 Active Spell Bar#

Only a limited number of Spells can be equipped at once, forcing players to shape an active kit rather than use every acquired option simultaneously.

  • The current prototype uses eight active Spell slots.
  • All other acquired Spells remain in the Spell Book.
  • Final-game and MVP swapping rules are defined in the Spell Book section above.
  • Auto Attacks remain available independently of equipped Spells.

Eight slots remain a Prototype / Under Evaluation value.

6.2.4 Spell Categories#

Spells are grouped according to their gameplay behavior rather than their color.

Current categories include:

  • Damage
  • Healing
  • Support
  • Crowd Control
  • Mobility
  • Defensive
  • Summoning
  • Utility

Individual Spells may belong to multiple categories simultaneously.

6.2.5 Spell Properties#

Every Spell is defined by a consistent set of properties.

Typical Spell properties include:

  • Name
  • Color School
  • Spell Category
  • Mana Cost
  • Cooldown
  • Cast Type
  • Target Type
  • Range
  • Duration
  • Damage or Healing values
  • Scaling
  • Conditions applied
  • Momentum interaction
  • Weakened interaction
  • Requirements

Using a consistent property set simplifies balancing and future content creation.

6.2.6 Cast Types#

Chapter 3 - Combat Systems is the canonical source for Instant, Channeled, Hard, Soft, and Mobile Cast definitions. Each Spell record identifies its Cast Type, cast time, movement behavior, and any Spell-specific interruption rules. New Cast Types should be added only when they create a genuinely distinct gameplay behavior.

6.2.7 Spell Scaling#

Most Spells increase in effectiveness through Character Statistics rather than fixed upgrades.

Depending on the Spell, scaling may include:

  • Weapon Damage
  • Physical Power
  • Spell Power
  • Healing Power
  • Weapon Critical Strike Chance
  • Momentum
  • Target Conditions
  • Talents
  • Equipment

Scaling allows Spells to remain relevant throughout an entire run without requiring constant replacement.

Spell Haste and Spell Critical Strike Chance are possible future Spell-scaling statistics. They remain under evaluation and are not Current Design.

6.2.8 Color Schools#

Every Spell has one Color School, which connects it to the identity rules in Chapter 7 - Color Schools.

Color determines the Spell's fantasy, mechanics and synergy with Talents, Conditions, Altars and future systems.

Current Color Schools:

  • Red
  • Blue
  • Green
  • Black
  • White

The design philosophy and identity of each Color School are described in Chapter 7 - Color Schools.

6.2.9 Spell Interactions#

Spells function independently but can interact by using shared systems and scaling methods.

Examples include:

  • Applying Weakened before consuming it.
  • Generating and consuming Momentum.
  • Using and scale through shared Conditions.
  • Combining Crowd Control effects.
  • Supporting allies through buffs and healing.
  • Building around specific Color Schools.
  • Overarching Talent improvement

These interactions form the foundation of Build creation and cooperative gameplay.

6.2.10 Open Variables#

The following systems remain under evaluation:

  • Maximum active Spell slots; eight is the current prototype.
  • Spell Ranks, rarity, Draft weighting, and replacement rules.
  • Future Spell Book filtering, sorting, and management.
  • Whether the collective Draft prayer may be attempted during combat.
  • Disconnects, dead players, interrupted channels, timeouts, forced completion, and multiple banked Draft handling.

6.2.11 Future Expansion#

Potential future additions include:

  • Spell Evolutions.
  • Spell Ranks.
  • Maybe through duplicates (3 ranks for each Spell, adept, veteran, master)
  • Additional Cast Types.
  • Passive Spells.
  • Ultimate abilities.
  • Spell modifications through Talents and Items.

Chapter 7 — Color Schools

7.1 Overview#

Color Schools are a defining system of DreamDungeon. Every Spell belongs to one of five Colors with a distinct fantasy, mechanical identity, and playstyle. Colors are not classes: players freely combine them to create varied Builds and party compositions. Their themes also influence Enemies, Bosses, Biomes, Altars, Talents, and other systems.

7.2 Current Design#

7.2.1 General Concept#

Every Spell belongs to exactly one Color School.

Color Schools determine the thematic identity of a Spell and form the foundation for interactions with Talents, Altars, Conditions and future systems.

Current design:

  • Every Spell belongs to one Color School.
  • Colors do not define character classes.
  • Players may freely combine Spells from different Colors.
  • Every Color has strengths and weaknesses.
  • Colors interact with Talents and future Altars.
  • Future Enemy factions and dungeon biomes are based around the Five Colors.

7.2.2 The Five Color Schools#

DreamDungeon currently consists of the following Color Schools:

  • Red – God of Rage
  • Blue – God of Delusion
  • Green – God of Cunning
  • Black – God of End
  • White – God of Mercy

Each Color represents one of the Five Old Gods and defines its own gameplay identity.

Chapter 8 — Conditions

8.1 Overview#

Conditions are temporary effects applied to players and Enemies during combat. They modify combat behavior, create interactions between Spells and provide the foundation for Build synergies. Some Conditions directly influence gameplay, while others primarily act as setup mechanics that enable additional Spell interactions.

8.2 Current Design#

General Concept

Conditions are applied through Spells, abilities, Talents and future items.

They serve several purposes:

  • Create interactions between different Spells.
  • Reward coordinated gameplay.
  • Strengthen Color School identity.
  • Increase Build diversity.
  • Expand Encounter design.

Conditions should always remain readable and easy to understand. Whenever possible, multiple Spells should interact with the same Condition rather than introducing new ones.

8.2.1 Condition Categories#

Conditions can generally be divided into four categories.

Offensive Conditions

Conditions that primarily deal damage or weaken Enemies over time.

Current examples:

  • Bleeding (physical damage over time; predominantly Green but not exclusive)
  • Burning
  • Curse
  • Leech
  • Poison (Future / Under Evaluation)

Bleeding and Poison

Bleeding represents sustained physical damage over time.

Current design:

  • Bleeding deals periodic physical damage. Poison requires a distinct approved purpose before implementation.
  • Can stack (under evaluation).
  • Bleeding is predominantly associated with Green but is not exclusive to it.

Burning

Burning represents magical fire damage over time.

Current design:

  • Deals periodic magical damage.
  • Primarily associated with Red.

Curse

Curses represent Black's primary debuff system.

Unlike Weakened, different Curses each provide unique gameplay effects.

Examples may include:

  • Reduced damage.
  • Reduced healing.
  • Reduced movement speed.
  • Increased damage taken.

Leech

Leech transfers Health from Enemies to the caster.

Current design:

  • Restores Health based on damage dealt.
  • Primarily associated with Black.

8.2.2 Crowd Control#

Conditions that directly limit Enemy actions.

Current examples:

  • Slow
  • Reduces movement speed by x%
  • Immobilize/Root
  • Reduces movement speed to 0. Disables mobility skills
  • Silence
  • Disables and Interrupts Spell casting
  • Disorient
  • Unit wanders around disabling all actions, awakens when damaged
  • Stun
  • Disables all actions, does not awaken when damaged
  • Blind
  • Reduced hit chance
  • Confuse
  • Unit attacks allies
  • Taunt
  • Unit is forced to attack caster
  • Fear
  • Unit runs away uncontrolably, may awaken when taken damage threshold is reached
  • Polymorph
  • Disables all actions, awakens when damaged, regenerates health while controled
  • Banish
  • Disables attacks and Spell casting, unit is slowed, cannot be targeted with Spells or attacked
  • Disarm
  • Cannot perform weapon attacks

Crowd Control is divided into Soft Crowd Control and Hard Crowd Control.

  • Soft Crowd Control reduces combat effectiveness while still allowing the affected unit to act.
  • Hard Crowd Control temporarily removes player or Enemy control almost entirely.

The detailed behavior of Crowd Control is described in Chapter 3 - Combat Systems.

8.2.3 Buffs#

Positive Conditions applied to allied units.

Current examples:

  • Blessings
  • Barriers
  • Haste
  • Weapon Enhancements
  • Aspects
  • Spirits

Buffs generally improve combat performance without directly affecting Enemies.

8.2.4 Setup Conditions#

Setup Conditions exist primarily to enable interactions with other Spells.

Current examples:

  • Weakened
  • Marked
  • Future Conditions

Unlike damage-over-time effects, Setup Conditions usually have little or no effect by themselves.

8.2.5 Weakened#

Weakened is an Enemy Condition and Combat Resource tag used as a cooperative setup state. It has no direct effect by itself. Chapter 5 - Resources is the canonical source for its stack, refresh, consumption, and numerical rules; this chapter defines how it participates in the reusable Condition framework.

8.2.6 Momentum#

Momentum is an individual Combat Resource that is also recognized by the Condition framework so Spells and presentation systems can tag and query it consistently. Chapter 5 - Resources is the canonical source for its generation, maximum, decay, consumption, and numerical behavior.

8.2.7 Condition Design Rules#

Every Condition requires one clearly defined purpose and a reusable record structure. The Technical GDD owns the shared engine behavior; the future Condition Bible owns complete individual records and values.

Reusable Condition properties include, where applicable:

  • Name, identifier, category, tags, and implementation status.
  • Valid targets and application source.
  • Duration, tick interval, stacking, maximum stacks, and refresh behavior.
  • Cleansing, immunity, resistance, and diminishing-return behavior.
  • Interactions, consumption rules, and associated Color Schools.
  • World-space and UI presentation requirements.
  • Multiplayer authority, replication, and synchronization behavior.

Conditions should be visually recognizable, avoid redundant purposes, support multiple Spells where useful, and create cooperation without unnecessary complexity.

8.2.8 Open Variables#

The following systems remain under evaluation:

  • Maximum Bleeding stacks and whether Poison has a distinct future purpose.
  • Maximum Bleeding stacks.
  • Curse implementation.
  • Future Buff categories.
  • Additional Setup Conditions.
  • Visual representation of Conditions.
  • Boss immunities and diminishing returns.

8.2.9 Future Expansion#

Potential future additions include:

  • Additional offensive Conditions.
  • Additional Blessings.
  • Additional Curses.
  • Environmental Conditions.
  • Equipment-specific Conditions.
  • Boss-exclusive Conditions.

Chapter 9 — Talents

9.1 Overview#

Talents are permanent Build enhancements acquired throughout a run. Unlike Spells, Talents modify existing gameplay rather than adding new active abilities. They allow players to specialize their Build, reinforce their chosen Color Schools or create synergies between different Colors. Together with Spells and Equipment, Talents form one of the three pillars of character progression.

9.2 Current Design#

9.2.1 General Concept#

Talents provide passive bonuses or modify existing gameplay systems.

Rather than granting additional active abilities, Talents strengthen the character's current Build by improving Spells, Conditions, resources or combat mechanics in an overarching manner.

Talents connect the systems within a Build, strengthen selected mechanics, and help players optimize the Spells they have acquired.

9.2.2 Talent Acquisition#

Talents are primarily obtained through the Altars of the Five Old Gods.

Players spend Relics earned from Boss Encounters to receive a Talent Draft.

Current design:

  • Bosses reward Relics.
  • Every player receives their own Relics.
  • Relics are spent individually at an Altar of the player's chosen Color.
  • Relics can be held to be turned in at another altar
  • Altars offer Talent Drafts.
  • Drafting occurs outside combat.

The exact frequency of Talent Drafts depends on Relic availability and dungeon progression.

9.2.3 Talent Drafts#

Talent Drafts are individual and substantially less frequent than Spell Drafts. The Planned MVP Prototype presents each player with three random Talent choices outside combat. The selected Talent remains active for the run and cannot currently be removed or replaced.

Draft frequency, weighting based on the current Build, rarity, and the final number of choices remain adjustable. Fixed Talent trees are Future / Under Evaluation only if testing shows that random Drafts provide insufficient player control.

9.2.4 Talent Categories#

Talents are divided into two primary categories.

Vertical Talents

Vertical Talents strengthen the mechanics of an existing Color School.

Examples include:

  • Increased Spell damage.
  • Improved healing.
  • Better Momentum generation.
  • Longer Buff duration.
  • Increased summon effectiveness.
  • Stronger defensive effects.

These Talents reinforce the player's current specialization.

Horizontal Talents

Horizontal Talents create interactions between multiple Color Schools.

Rather than increasing raw power, they reward combining different mechanics.

Examples include:

  • Red abilities benefiting from Weakened.
  • Blue Spells generating Momentum.
  • Black Curses improving White Blessings.
  • Green Marks increasing summon damage.
  • White healing triggering effects on Red abilities.

Horizontal Talents are intended to increase Build diversity without forcing specific combinations.

9.2.5 Talent Design Rules#

Every Talent should follow a consistent design philosophy.

Current rules:

  • Modify existing gameplay instead of creating entirely new systems.
  • Have overarching benefits within or beyond one Color School
  • Be understandable without requiring complex explanations.
  • Support one or more Color Schools.
  • Encourage different Build paths.
  • Avoid mandatory choices.
  • Be useful immediately after acquisition.

Talents should feel impactful without overshadowing Spell Drafts or Equipment.

9.2.6 Talent Interaction#

Talents may interact with multiple gameplay systems simultaneously.

Current interactions include:

  • Spells
  • Conditions
  • Combat resources (Momentum, Weakened)
  • Resources
  • Equipment
  • Summons
  • Color Schools

A major part of late game scaling is expected to come from these interactions.

9.2.7 Build Identity#

Together with Spells and Equipment, Talents shape the player's final Build.

A complete Build is expected to emerge through the interaction of:

  • Active Spells
  • Equipment
  • Talents
  • Conditions
  • Resources

No single system should completely define a character.

9.2.8 Design Goals#

The Talent system is intended to:

  • Reward long-term planning.
  • Reinforce different playstyles.
  • Encourage experimentation.
  • Increase replayability.
  • Create meaningful choices.
  • Strengthen Color School identity.

9.2.9 Open Variables#

The following systems remain under evaluation:

  • Talent Draft frequency.
  • Number of Talents per draft.
  • Draft weighting and whether testing eventually justifies an alternative to random Drafts.
  • Neutral Talent distribution.
  • Draft weighting.
  • Vertical versus Horizontal ratio.
  • Talent rarity.
  • Neutral Talents.

9.2.10 Future Expansion#

Potential future additions include:

No additional Talent expansion is committed. New ideas should be added only after explicit approval.

Chapter 10 — Equipment

10.1 Overview#

Equipment is one of the three primary progression systems in DreamDungeon, alongside Spells and Talents. It provides the majority of a character's numerical power by increasing Character Statistics and enabling different Build paths. Unlike Talents, Equipment primarily affects a character's attributes rather than introducing new mechanics, allowing players to continuously strengthen and refine their Build throughout a run.

10.2 Current Design#

10.2.1 General Concept#

Equipment represents a character's wearable items acquired throughout a run.

Its primary purpose is to increase Character Statistics, strengthen Auto Attacks and improve the effectiveness of Spells through stat scaling.

Equipment is intended to provide continuous character growth between Spell Drafts and Talent Drafts.

10.2.2 Equipment Acquisition#

Equipment is acquired throughout a run from Enemies, Bosses, Shops, treasure locations, Points of Interest, and special Encounters.

The current prototype uses personal loot boxes for Uncommon quality and below and group loot rolls for Rare quality and above. Higher-quality Equipment is more strongly associated with Bosses and later progression. Floor drops, smart loot, Boss-specific tables, and alternative distribution systems remain Open Variables.

Drops should be frequent enough to create continuous progression but uncommon enough that an item normally represents a meaningful potential upgrade.

10.2.3 Equipment Slots#

The current prototype uses the following non-Weapon Equipment slots:

  • Head
  • Shoulders
  • Chest
  • Gloves
  • Legs
  • Boots
  • Ring
  • Necklace
  • Trinket

Main Hand, Off Hand, and a possible separate Ranged slot depend on the open Weapon-model decision in Chapter 3 - Combat Systems. Bracers, Belt, a second Ring, and a second Trinket are removed from the current prototype so upgrade moments are not spread across too many slots.

10.2.4 Character Statistics#

Equipment supplies most direct numerical character growth by adding or modifying Character Statistics. Chapter 5 - Resources contains the canonical Character Statistics list; this chapter defines how Equipment rolls, displays, and distributes those values.

10.2.5 Primary Stats (under evaluation)#

Primary Statistics are an Open Variable intended to improve item readability by combining several direct Statistics under broader labels. Proposed mappings include:

Primary StatisticProposed effects
StrengthPhysical Power and Block Chance
AgilityWeapon Critical Strike Chance and Dodge Chance
IntellectSpell Power, Magic Resistance, and potentially future Spell Critical Strike Chance
WillpowerHealing Power and Magic Resistance
StaminaHealth
WisdomMana

These mappings are not yet Current Design and require implementation and balance evaluation.

10.2.6 Item Quality#

The Planned MVP Prototype uses four Equipment qualities:

  • Common
  • Uncommon
  • Rare
  • Epic

Higher quality generally provides stronger Statistics or additional affix capacity. Legendary Equipment and distinctive Legendary mechanics remain future development. Drop rates, thresholds, and exact quality effects remain adjustable.

10.2.7 Affixes#

MVP Equipment primarily uses direct Character Statistics as readable affixes, including Armor, Shield Block Amount, Health Regeneration, Mana Regeneration, and Attack Speed. Most Equipment should remain statistical and easy to compare.

Distinctive passive effects, sets, unique mechanics, and Legendary effects remain later development so Equipment supports rather than replaces Spell and Talent identity.

10.2.8 Equipment Trading#

Equipment may be traded freely between party members outside combat and cannot be traded during Encounters. This supports cooperative allocation and allows a party to prioritize overall strength rather than individual ownership.

The final interaction method remains open: a direct trade window, designated trade tables, or dropping an item for another player are all candidates. Whatever method is selected must preserve the outside-combat restriction and remain understandable in multiplayer.

10.2.9 Equipment Progression#

Equipment provides steady progression throughout a run.

Equipment upgrades occur more frequently than Spell or Talent Drafts and generally provide smaller incremental gains. Drops should still feel meaningful; Equipment should not be replaced so often that individual items lose their identity.

Together with Spells and Talents, Equipment completes the three primary progression pillars.

10.2.10 Build Interaction#

Equipment interacts with multiple gameplay systems.

Current interactions include:

  • Character Statistics
  • Auto Attacks
  • Spell Scaling
  • Conditions
  • Resources
  • Talents

Equipment should complement a player's current Build rather than completely redefine it.

10.2.11 Design Goals#

The Equipment system is intended to:

  • Provide continuous progression.
  • Encourage cooperative item distribution.
  • Reward exploration and Encounters.
  • Support a wide variety of Builds.
  • Reinforce existing gameplay systems.
  • Create meaningful upgrade decisions.

10.2.12 Open Variables#

The following systems remain under evaluation:

  • Gear drop rate and item-level scaling.
  • Primary Statistics and their proposed mappings.
  • Smart loot versus unrestricted random loot.
  • Spell Haste as an under-evaluation Character Statistic affecting cast times and periodic tick intervals.
  • Spell Critical Strike Chance as a separate under-evaluation statistic from Weapon Critical Strike Chance.
  • Fixed Boss tables versus random Boss loot.
  • Floor drops and other alternatives to the current mixed loot prototype.
  • Unique Item implementation.
  • Trading interaction: direct window, trade table, or dropping an item for another player.

10.2.13 Future Expansion#

Potential future additions include:

  • Set Items.
  • Unique Items.
  • Crafted Equipment.
  • Color-specific Equipment.
  • Boss-exclusive Equipment.
  • Equipment upgrading.

Chapter 11 — Enemy Design

11.1 Overview#

Enemies are the primary challenge players face throughout a run. They create the moment-to-moment gameplay experience, reward progression through combat and continuously teach players how to interact with DreamDungeon's combat systems. Rather than functioning as isolated opponents, Enemies are designed to complement one another within Encounters, encouraging cooperation, target prioritization and tactical decision-making.

11.2 Current Design#

11.2.1 General Concept#

Enemies are designed around clearly defined combat roles rather than individual complexity.

Every Enemy should fulfill a specific purpose within an Encounter and contribute to the overall challenge by interacting with other Enemies. Difficulty should primarily emerge from Enemy combinations instead of overly complicated individual mechanics.

Enemy design should follow the same philosophy as player design:

  • Simple and readable individual mechanics.
  • Strong interactions with other systems.
  • High replayability through combinations.
  • Easily understandable behavior.
  • Clear visual identity.

11.2.2 Enemy Design Philosophy#

Every Enemy should follow a number of core design principles.

Current principles:

  • Every Enemy has a clearly defined combat role.
  • Every Enemy teaches the player something.
  • Behavior should be readable and somewhat understandable but punish mistakes.
  • Visual readability always takes priority over complexity.
  • Encounters should be more difficult than individual Enemies.
  • Enemy abilities should reinforce their Color School identity.

Players should quickly recognize what an Enemy does without having to memorize complex ability descriptions.

11.2.3 Enemy Roles#

Every Enemy has one readable primary combat purpose and may combine several role labels when needed.

Roles define an Enemy's purpose within an Encounter rather than restricting the abilities it may possess.

Current roles include:

BrawlerFrontline damage Enemy. Walks into melee and pressures players.
TankDurable frontline Enemy that protects allies or controls space.
RangedDeals consistent ranged damage from distance.
HealerRestores health or prevents death of allies.
SupportBuffs allies or debuffs players.
ControllerUses crowd control, displacement, slows, roots, silence.
AssassinHunts vulnerable or isolated players.
SummonerCreates additional units or objects.
ArtilleryUses slow, dangerous AoE attacks.

Tank

Purpose:

  • Protect vulnerable allies.
  • Occupy space.
  • Needs to be dealt eventually
  • Prevent access to ranged Enemies.

Typical characteristics:

  • High Health.
  • High Armor.
  • Low mobility.
  • Moderate damage.

Brawler

Purpose:

  • Deal sustained or burst melee damage.
  • Creates immediate pressure that must be addressed.

Typical characteristics:

  • Medium survivability.
  • High damage.
  • Moderate mobility.
  • Limited utility.

Ranged

Purpose:

  • Pressure players from a distance with physical or magical damage
  • Force movement.

Typical characteristics:

  • Low survivability.
  • High range.
  • High damage.
  • Vulnerable when isolated.

Healer

Purpose:

  • Heal damaged Enemy allies

Typical abilities include:

  • Healing.
  • Shields.
  • Removing Conditions.
  • Low Damage.
  • Low mobility.
  • Low survivability.

Support Enemies should become valuable priority targets without becoming mandatory every Encounter.

Support

Purpose:

  • Increase the effectiveness of allied Enemies.

Typical abilities include:

  • Buffs
  • Removing Conditions.
  • Medium Survivability
  • Low damage
  • Low mobility

Support Enemies should become valuable priority targets without becoming mandatory every Encounter.

Controller

Purpose:

  • Restrict player actions.
  • Control battlefield

Typical abilities include:

  • Crowd Control.
  • Area denial.
  • Pulls and knockbacks.
  • Resource disruption.
  • Medium Damage
  • Medium survivability
  • Medium to high mobility.

Summoner

Purpose:

  • Increase Encounter pressure over time.

Typical mechanics include:

  • Summoning reinforcements.
  • Reviving defeated allies.
  • Creating temporary creatures.
  • Spawning environmental hazards.
  • High Damage (through summons)
  • Medium survivability
  • Low mobility

Summoners reward quick target prioritization before Encounters become overwhelming.

Assassin

Purpose:

  • Focus Backlines
  • Elude threat mechanics

Typical mechanics include:

  • Invisibility/Camouflage
  • Higher Threat from Healers
  • Focus low survivability players
  • High Damage
  • Medium Survivability
  • High Mobility

Artillery

Purpose:

  • Uses slow, dangerous AoE attacks.
  • Punishes bad positioning

Typical mechanics include:

  • Extremely high area-of-effect damage
  • Avoidable through positioning
  • Interruptible
  • Low to no mobility
  • Low to medium survivability

11.2.4 Enemy Behavior#

Enemy behavior is controled through a specific AI Framework.

Every Enemy follows the same fundamental behavior structure while individual behaviors determine how each Enemy acts.

Enemy behavior is assembled from triggers and actions. Enemy Abilities and Spells may also activate specific behavior responses.

Additional behavior nodes may be added as new Enemy mechanics are introduced.

Current triggers and actions are currently saved in the DreamDungeon AI Framework sheet.

11.2.5 AI Design#

The AI should create believable opponents without relying on unfair advantages.

Current AI goals:

  • Prioritize combat role specific targets.
  • Interact with allied Enemies in certain ways taking into consideration the combat roles
  • React consistently.
  • Avoid random behavior.
  • Use abilities intelligently (through triggers)
  • Cooperate with nearby Enemies.

Players should gradually learn Enemy behavior through repeated Encounters.

11.2.6 Enemy Scaling#

Enemy difficulty should grow primarily through mechanics and Encounter composition. For readability and content reuse, Enemies also use temporary development quality labels - Normal, Elite, Mini Boss, and Boss - plus level-based statistical scaling within those categories. Higher levels allow suitable Enemies to reappear later in a run.

Scaling may include:

  • Increased Statistics
  • More dangerous Encounter compositions.
  • Additional abilities.
  • Improved cooperation.

11.2.7 Color Identity#

Most Enemies use a Color identity, but wildlife, universal Guardians, tutorial units, and other suitable designs may be Neutral.

Enemy Colors determine:

  • General theme
  • Spell selection.
  • Conditions.
  • Behavior.
  • Visual identity.
  • Future biome placement.

Enemies should clearly communicate their Color through both visuals and gameplay.

11.2.8 Elite Enemies#

Elite Enemies are stronger variants of regular Enemies.

Current characteristics:

  • Increased survivability.
  • Additional abilities.
  • Improved AI behavior.
  • Greater rewards.
  • Mini-Boss potential.

Elite Enemies are encouraged, but not required, to add a meaningful mechanical or behavioral difference. A purely statistical Elite is permitted when it serves the Encounter and remains readable.

11.2.9 Enemy Readability#

Enemy behavior should always remain easy to understand.

Current design guidelines:

  • Clear silhouettes.
  • Level Indicator
  • Distinct animations.
  • Readable Spell effects.
  • Visible cast indicators (Enemy cast bar) and animations
  • Consistent visual language.
  • Minimal visual clutter.

Players should quickly understand why they succeeded or failed during an Encounter.

11.2.10 Design Goals#

Enemy design aims to:

  • Encourage teamwork.
  • Reward target prioritization.
  • Reward intelligent Crowd Control chains and target prioritization.
  • Promote movement.
  • Support varied Encounters.
  • Reinforce Color identity.
  • Teach mechanics naturally.
  • Remain readable in large Encounters.

11.2.11 Open Variables#

The following systems remain under evaluation:

  • Final Enemy role count.
  • Behavior Tree complexity.
  • UI Elements for readability - (clearly indicated Spell list, behaviors)
  • Elite Enemy frequency.
  • Enemy affixes.
  • Enemy scaling curve.
  • Shared abilities between Enemy families.
  • Target prioritization logic.

11.2.12 Future Expansion#

Potential future additions include:

  • Enemy families.
  • Rare roaming Enemies.
  • Color-specific elite modifiers.
  • Environmental interactions.
  • Adaptive AI behaviors.
  • Dynamic Encounter modifiers.

Chapter 12 — Encounter Design

12.1 Overview#

Encounters are the primary gameplay units of DreamDungeon. Rather than challenging players through individual Enemies, Encounters combine different Enemy roles, mechanics and environmental elements to create engaging combat scenarios. Every Encounter should encourage communication, target prioritization and tactical decision-making while continuously teaching players new mechanics throughout a run.

12.2 Current Design#

12.2.1 General Concept#

An Encounter is an authored development record that groups one or more Enemy Groups into an intended tactical challenge within a Section. It may reference the Combat Areas, environmental challenges, completion conditions and rewards designed for that challenge, but it does not create a hard spatial or gameplay boundary. Where the level permits, players may combine groups, disengage or skip an Encounter.

The challenge of an Encounter is determined by the combination of Enemies, their behaviors and the environment rather than by the strength of individual Enemies.

Encounters should feel authored and readable. MVP Encounters are handcrafted; the final game may procedurally recombine validated Enemy Groups and Encounter records under explicit counterplay and readability constraints.

Current design principles:

  • Enemy combinations create difficulty.
  • Encounters gradually increase in complexity.
  • Mechanics should Build upon previously learned mechanics.
  • Every Encounter should remain readable.

12.2.2 Encounter Composition#

Encounters are built by combining Enemies with complementary combat roles.

An Encounter does not need every Enemy role and may include several Enemies with the same role when that composition creates a readable tactical purpose.

Different combinations create different tactical challenges while keeping individual Enemies relatively simple.

12.2.3 Encounter Types#

Encounter labels support authoring, filtering, difficulty, and rewards without creating hard gameplay boundaries.

Standard Encounters teach and reinforce mechanics, reward progression, and Build player confidence.

Elite Encounters contain one or more Elite Enemies, increase difficulty and rewards, and may introduce more advanced interactions without reaching Boss complexity.

Boss Encounters are major milestones with unique mechanics and progression checkpoints. Multiple phases are optional. Most Boss Encounters grant significant rewards; the MVP End Boss is the explicit no-progression-loot exception described in Chapter 13 - Boss Design.

Special Encounters remain Future / Under Evaluation and may include survival, escort, puzzle, timed, or arena-challenge structures.

12.2.4 Difficulty Progression#

Encounter difficulty should increase gradually throughout a run.

Difficulty may increase through:

  • Increased Enemy level.
  • Increased Enemy quality (normal, elite, mini Boss, Boss).
  • More Enemies.
  • New Enemy roles.
  • Improved Enemy combinations.
  • Additional mechanics.
  • More demanding positioning.

Players should feel that they are learning rather than simply facing Enemies with larger Health pools.

12.2.5 Teaching Mechanics#

One of the primary goals of Encounter design is to teach players how the game works.

Current design philosophy:

  • Introduce one new concept at a time.
  • Reinforce previously learned mechanics.
  • Combine familiar mechanics in new ways.
  • Avoid overwhelming the player.
  • Reward observation and adaptation.

Later Encounters should naturally Build upon earlier lessons.

12.2.6 Combat Area Design#

Chapter 14 - Level Design owns general Combat Area layout types and their placement within Sections. Encounter records add only the tactical requirements needed by their Enemy combination, including area size, line of sight, obstacles, elevation, environmental hazards, and spawn locations. These elements should support counterplay without making an Encounter frustrating or unreadable.

12.2.7 Reward Structure#

The Technical GDD defines the general completion and reward framework. Each authored Encounter record owns its exact completion checks and attached Experience, Gold, Equipment, Relics, and other rewards. Skipped or incomplete Encounters do not grant their attached rewards.

Reward value and quality should reflect difficulty and route opportunity cost. Chapter 13 - Boss Design defines Boss-specific milestones and the MVP End Boss exception.

12.2.8 Replayability#

Replayability comes from validated variation rather than uncontrolled randomness. The final game may recombine authored Enemy Groups, Encounter records, levels, Elite variants, and Combat Area contexts under explicit readability and counterplay rules. MVP Encounters remain handcrafted.

Dynamic modifiers, random events, and additional procedural systems remain later development.

12.2.9 Design Goals#

Encounter design aims to:

  • Encourage teamwork.
  • Reward communication.
  • Promote target prioritization.
  • Teach mechanics naturally.
  • Create memorable combat situations.
  • Maintain pacing throughout a run.

12.2.10 Open Variables#

The following systems remain under evaluation:

  • Encounter length.
  • Enemy density.
  • Elite Encounter frequency.
  • Reward pacing.
  • Special Encounter frequency.
  • Dynamic Encounter modifiers.

12.2.11 Future Expansion#

Potential future additions include:

  • Ambush Encounters.
  • Environmental puzzles.
  • Dynamic events and modifiers.
  • Optional challenge variants beyond the core challenge-location framework.
  • Endless Mode Encounter variants.

Procedural recombination of validated Encounter records and core Point-of-Interest challenge locations belong to the final-game Current Design.

Chapter 13 — Boss Design

13.1 Overview#

Bosses are major run milestones that test previously learned mechanics through coordination, positioning, and adaptation. They prioritize unique, readable Encounter challenges over simple statistical inflation. Multiple phases are optional and should be used only when they strengthen the Boss fantasy or tactical escalation.

13.2 Current Design#

13.2.1 General Concept#

Bosses are unique Encounters designed around one or more central gameplay mechanics.

Unlike standard Enemies, Bosses are intended to challenge the entire party through mechanics, positioning and coordination rather than simply acting as stronger Enemies.

Bosses should represent significant progression milestones throughout a run.

Current design principles:

  • Every Boss has a unique identity.
  • Every Boss teaches or tests specific mechanics.
  • Bosses prioritize mechanics.
  • Bosses have increased statistics
  • Bosses should remain readable despite their complexity.
  • Every Boss Encounter should feel memorable.

13.2.2 Boss Progression MVP#

In the MVP, Mini Bosses appear at Encounters 5, 10, and 15. They award Experience, Gold, higher-quality Equipment, and individually owned Relics. Exact quantities and loot tables remain adjustable.

The End Boss appears at Encounter 20 and ends the run. Because progression no longer supports the completed run, it grants no Experience, Gold, Equipment, or Relics. Victory instead triggers results, presentation, and completion records.

Final-game Boss placement follows the authored Act, Dungeon, Section, and route structure rather than the fixed MVP interval.

13.2.3 Boss Identity#

Every Boss should be built around a unique fantasy.

Boss identity consists of:

  • Color School.
  • Theme.
  • Visual identity.
  • Combat mechanics.
  • Phases.
  • Behavior.
  • Arena design.
  • Encounter gimmick.

A player should immediately recognize a Boss through both its appearance and gameplay.

13.2.4 Boss Mechanics#

Every Boss should introduce mechanics that differentiate it from normal Encounters.

Possible mechanics include:

  • Area denial.
  • Summoning additional Enemies.
  • Target swapping.
  • Positioning challenges.
  • Environmental hazards.
  • Interrupt mechanics.
  • Soft enrage mechanics.
  • Cooperative objectives.

Boss mechanics should encourage teamwork and individual execution.

13.2.5 Boss Phases#

Bosses may use multiple combat phases when phases strengthen fantasy, escalation, or tactical learning. A strong single-phase Boss or Mini Boss is valid.

Phases allow Encounters to evolve over time while introducing new mechanics gradually.

Possible phase transitions include:

  • Health thresholds.
  • Time-based transitions.
  • Environmental changes.
  • Additional Enemy waves.
  • Arena transformations.

Each phase should Build naturally upon previous mechanics rather than replacing them completely.

13.2.6 Arena Design#

Chapter 14 - Level Design owns general Combat Area layout rules. A Boss record defines only its unique arena requirements, such as movement space, line of sight, hazards, spawn locations, and phase-specific changes. The arena should actively reinforce the Boss mechanics rather than serve as a generic room.

13.2.7 Boss Difficulty#

Boss difficulty should primarily arise from execution rather than numerical scaling.

Difficulty may increase through:

  • Additional mechanics.
  • Faster ability combinations.
  • More demanding positioning.
  • Greater coordination requirements.
  • Increased mechanical overlap.

Pure Health inflation should be minimized whenever possible.

13.2.8 Boss AI#

Boss behavior follows the same underlying AI framework as regular Enemies while introducing additional Encounter-specific behavior.

Boss AI may include:

  • Phase transitions.
  • Ability rotations.
  • Trigger-based mechanics.
  • Summon logic.
  • Target prioritization.
  • Environmental interaction.

Boss behavior should remain consistent enough for players to learn through repeated attempts.

13.2.9 Rewards#

Mini Bosses and Bosses normally provide the most valuable progression rewards within a run, including Experience, Gold, higher-quality Equipment, and Relics where specified by their Encounter records. The MVP End Boss is the explicit exception: it ends the run and therefore grants completion results rather than progression loot.

13.2.10 Design Goals#

Boss design aims to:

  • Test previously learned mechanics.
  • Reward communication.
  • Encourage coordinated gameplay.
  • Create memorable Encounters.
  • Reinforce Color School identity.
  • Deliver satisfying progression milestones.

13.2.11 Open Variables#

The following systems remain under evaluation:

  • Boss Encounter frequency.
  • Boss placement in open levels.
  • Fixed Boss loot or random loot.
  • Number of Boss phases.
  • Boss-specific affixes.
  • Arena interaction complexity.
  • Random Boss mechanics.
  • Scaling for different party sizes.

13.2.12 Future Expansion#

Potential future additions include:

  • Optional Bosses.
  • Secret Bosses.
  • Multi-Boss Encounters.
  • Endless mode Bosses.
  • Biome-specific Boss variants.

Chapter 14 — Level Design

14.1 Overview#

Level Design defines how players move through a run, how Encounters are presented, and how exploration supports progression. The final game contains six Acts, each with one Dungeon assembled from branching authored Sections, Combat Areas, Points of Interest, and coherent Biome or Color influences. The MVP uses a Central Hub and one handcrafted Combat Arena as a reduced validation structure.

14.2 Current Design#

14.2.1 General Concept#

Level Design provides the framework in which all gameplay systems interact.

Rather than existing purely as visual environments, levels should actively support combat, exploration, progression and player decision-making.

Every level should encourage players to make meaningful choices while maintaining a clear sense of progression.

Current design principles:

  • Gameplay before visuals.
  • Readable layouts.
  • Support combat rather than restrict it.
  • Encourage exploration.
  • Reward curiosity.
  • Reinforce biome identity.

14.2.2 MVP Structure#

The MVP uses a simplified level structure to validate the game's core systems before implementing procedural dungeon generation.

Current design:

  • Central Hub with Shop and well.
  • One combat arena.

The Hub acts as the connection between all Encounters, allowing players to prepare for the next Combat Area without interruptions during combat.

14.2.3 Final Dungeon Structure#

The final game uses six Acts. Each Act contains one Dungeon, and each Dungeon is assembled from multiple branching authored Sections. Generation arranges validated Sections, paths, Combat Areas, Enemy Groups, and Points of Interest under explicit rules rather than generating unrestricted raw geometry.

Final-game Dungeons include branching routes, Combat Areas, Boss Encounters, Points of Interest, hidden treasures, and coherent biome or Color transitions. Deliberate blocked routes and points of no return prevent parties from clearing every branch, making route choice, available rewards, and progression meaningful.

14.2.4 The Six Acts#

The current design contains six Acts, each with one Dungeon and its own layout, Mini Bosses, and required Dungeon Boss. Defeating the Dungeon Boss unlocks the next Act. Act VI ends with the final Boss and game victory; Endless Mode remains future potential.

  1. Surface Haven
  2. Canyon Descent
  3. Cave Threshold
  4. Living Depths
  5. Cinder Warrens
  6. Deepest Dark

The Dungeon Visual Language Guide is the canonical visual and palette reference.

14.2.5 Dynamic Architecture#

Although the DreamDungeon is experienced as a physical dungeon, its structure is not fixed.

The layout of the dungeon continuously changes between expeditions, preventing explorers from memorizing routes or creating reliable maps. Corridors may connect differently, passages may disappear and entire sections of the dungeon may shift into new configurations.

To the inhabitants of the world, this phenomenon is simply accepted as one of the Dungeon's many mysteries.

Unknown to the player, these changes are a natural consequence of the Dungeon's true nature. Since the DreamDungeon is ultimately shaped by the subconscious of the Ancient One, its architecture constantly reorganizes itself in ways that resemble thoughts, memories and emotions rather than conventional physical spaces.

Despite this hidden explanation, the Dungeon should always feel believable and grounded. The player should experience it as an authentic dungeon rather than an abstract dreamscape. The constantly changing layout exists to reinforce exploration, replayability and the feeling that no expedition is ever exactly the same.

14.2.6 Combat Areas#

Combat Areas are the spatial foundation for Encounters within authored Sections. Chapter 14 - Level Design owns their general layout language and connection to routes; Chapters 12 and 13 define Encounter-specific and Boss-specific tactical requirements.

Combat Areas may include open rooms, narrow corridors, bridges, caverns, courtyards, Boss chambers, and natural clearings. Elevation and environmental hazards are intended final-game tools, while their exact implementation and frequency remain Open Variables.

14.2.7 Biomes#

Biomes and Color influence communicate identity through visual style, Enemy selection, music, environmental storytelling, and mechanics. Sections may be Neutral, influenced by one Color, or influenced by several Colors. A Dungeon may contain multiple coherent stretches with different influences rather than assigning every location to exactly one Old God.

14.2.8 Branching Paths#

Players are expected to make strategic route decisions throughout a run. There should be a point of no return mechanic so not the whole dungeon can be cleared and certain parts need to be left out strategically.

Possible route choices may include:

  • Easier Encounters.
  • Elite Encounters.
  • Boss progression.
  • Shops.
  • Altars.
  • Points of Interest.
  • Hidden treasures.
  • Future harvestable Resources, potentially tied to Biomes.

Route planning should become an important part of long-term strategy.

14.2.9 Points of Interest#

Core Points of Interest provide optional decisions outside standard combat:

  • Shops
  • Wells
  • Altars of the Five Old Gods
  • Treasure locations
  • Challenge locations

Quests, lore locations, trading stations, temporary-buff shrines, blacksmiths, enchanters, and herbalists remain Future / Under Evaluation. Not every run should contain the same Points of Interest.

14.2.10 Environmental Storytelling#

The world should communicate its history through its environments rather than lengthy exposition.

Examples include:

  • Ruined architecture.
  • Ancient battlefields.
  • Forgotten shrines.
  • Corrupted forests.
  • Abandoned settlements.
  • Remains of previous adventurers.

Environmental storytelling should reinforce the mystery of DreamDungeon without interrupting gameplay.

14.2.11 Level Design Goals#

Level Design aims to:

  • Support combat.
  • Encourage exploration.
  • Reward route planning.
  • Reinforce biome identity.
  • Create memorable locations.
  • Balance freedom with readability.

14.2.12 Open Variables#

The following systems remain under evaluation:

  • Dungeon floor size.
  • Branching path complexity.
  • Point of no return mechanic
  • Point of Interest and their frequency.
  • Secret area generation.
  • Environmental hazards.
  • Procedural generation rules.
  • Biome transition pacing.

14.2.13 Future Expansion#

Potential future additions include:

  • Dynamic environmental events.
  • Hidden shortcuts.
  • Puzzle rooms.
  • Additional interactive environmental mechanics.
  • Endless Dungeon generation.

Authored procedural Section assembly, elevation, and environmental hazards belong to the intended final-game Current Design rather than Future Expansion.

Chapter 15 — Setting

15.1 Overview#

The Setting provides the world in which DreamDungeon takes place. Its primary purpose is to support gameplay by creating a coherent atmosphere, recognizable locations and a believable world. While players experience DreamDungeon as a traditional fantasy dungeon crawler, the world hides a much deeper truth that is gradually uncovered throughout the story.

The setting should always reinforce exploration, mystery and progression without overshadowing the core gameplay.

15.2 Current Design#

15.2.1 General Concept#

DreamDungeon is set in a dark fantasy world shaped by the influence of the Five Old Gods and an ancient creator known throughout history as the New God.

Most inhabitants of the world believe the Five Old Gods have always ruled creation and that the New God is a corrupt divine being who threatens the balance of the world.

Whether this belief is true remains one of the central mysteries of the game.

Current design principles:

  • Gameplay before narrative.
  • Mystery over exposition.
  • Environmental storytelling.
  • Strong atmosphere.
  • Worldbuilding through exploration.
  • The player should continuously question what is true.

15.2.2 The World#

The physical world exists outside the DreamDungeon.

Ancient civilizations, forgotten kingdoms and generations of history have shaped the lands above, but very little of that history is fully understood.

Most historical records have been lost, altered or deliberately forgotten over thousands of years.

As a result, even scholars disagree on the true origins of the Five Old Gods and the New God.

The player begins the game accepting the world's common beliefs without questioning them.

15.2.3 The DreamDungeon#

The DreamDungeon is the central location of the game.

To the player, it appears to be an enormous ancient dungeon stretching deep beneath the surface of the world.

Every run represents another descent into its depths.

Although the Dungeon behaves like a physical place, its architecture constantly changes between runs.

This phenomenon is accepted by the world's inhabitants but never fully explained during the early stages of the game.

The Dungeon should feel:

  • Ancient.
  • Dangerous.
  • Mysterious.
  • Alive.
  • Worth exploring.
  • Constantly changing.

At first, these changes appear to be natural properties of the Dungeon itself.

Only much later does the player discover their true origin.

15.2.4 The Hidden Truth - Setting Context#

Unknown to both the player and most inhabitants of the world, the DreamDungeon is not a physical dungeon.

It is the subconscious mind of the Ancient One.

The Five Old Gods guide chosen mortals into this subconscious through an ancient ritual, allowing them to unknowingly enter a dreamlike state.

The player is never explicitly told this at the beginning of the game.

Instead, subtle clues are scattered throughout the adventure.

This is the concise Setting premise. Chapter 16 - Lore owns the historical truth, while Chapter 18 - Story owns how the player discovers the revelation.

15.2.5 The Guardians#

The Dungeon is protected by countless Guardians. Guardian is a narrative label for an Enemy; all Guardian records use the Enemy systems and schemas defined in Chapter 11 - Enemy Design.

To the player, they appear to be monsters, cultists, beasts and ancient defenders.

In reality, many are distorted manifestations of memories, fears and beings that have become bound to the Ancient One's subconscious.

Their appearance should still feel grounded within the dark fantasy setting.

The dream origin of these creatures should remain subtle and should never dominate their visual identity.

15.2.6 Atmosphere#

The overall atmosphere should remain grounded in traditional dungeon exploration.

Current themes include:

  • Mystery.
  • Wonder.
  • Ancient civilizations.
  • Decay.
  • Corruption.
  • Discovery.
  • Isolation.
  • Forgotten history.

The player should feel they are exploring an ancient dungeon—not an abstract dreamscape.

Dreamlike elements should exist primarily as subtle inconsistencies rather than overt visual surrealism.

Examples include:

  • Altered layouts between runs.
  • Familiar locations appearing differently.
  • Environmental details that subtly change.
  • Dialogue that hints at previous descents.

These elements should become meaningful only in hindsight after later story revelations.

15.2.7 Biomes#

The DreamDungeon consists of multiple distinct regions influenced by the Five Old Gods.

Each biome communicates its identity through:

  • Architecture.
  • Vegetation.
  • Enemy factions.
  • Color School.
  • Combat mechanics.
  • Environmental storytelling.
  • Ambient audio.

Biomes should remain believable as physical locations despite their hidden dreamlike nature.

15.2.8 Exploration#

Exploration rewards curiosity while gradually revealing fragments of the world's forgotten history.

Players may discover:

  • Hidden treasures.
  • Points of Interest.
  • Ancient records.
  • Forgotten temples.
  • Secret passages.
  • Lore fragments.
  • Optional Encounters.

The majority of lore should be discovered indirectly through exploration rather than lengthy dialogue.

15.2.9 Visual Identity#

The visual language of DreamDungeon should prioritize grounded fantasy.

Current goals:

  • Ancient architecture.
  • Distinct Color School identities.
  • High gameplay readability.
  • Memorable landmarks.
  • Organic environmental storytelling.
  • Subtle dreamlike inconsistencies.

The dream aspect of the setting should explain the world rather than define its appearance.

Players should primarily experience DreamDungeon as a believable fantasy dungeon.

15.2.10 Design Goals#

The Setting aims to:

  • Support gameplay through atmosphere.
  • Preserve the feeling of classic dungeon exploration.
  • Reinforce mystery without confusing the player.
  • Encourage exploration.
  • Gradually reveal the world's hidden truth.
  • Create memorable locations.
  • Maintain a coherent visual identity.

15.2.11 Open Variables#

The following systems remain under evaluation:

  • The complete history of the Ancient One.
  • The origin of the Five Old Gods.
  • The exact dreamwalking ritual.
  • The player's role before becoming a Dream Walker.
  • The relationship between the physical world and the DreamDungeon.
  • The extent to which the Ancient One is aware of the player's presence.

15.2.12 Future Expansion#

Potential future additions include:

  • Dream Walker NPCs.
  • Reality distortions during major story moments.

Chapter 16 — Lore

16.1 Overview#

Lore provides the historical foundation of DreamDungeon. Unlike the Setting, which describes the world as the player experiences it, Lore explains the events that shaped it. Much of this history has been forgotten, altered or deliberately concealed over thousands of years. As a result, neither the player nor most inhabitants of the world possess a complete understanding of the past.

16.2 Current Design#

16.2.1 General Concept#

The history of DreamDungeon is intentionally fragmented.

Rather than presenting the complete truth from the beginning, the player gradually uncovers pieces of the world's past through exploration, environmental storytelling, optional discoveries and major story events.

The Lore should continuously encourage players to question their understanding of history.

Current design principles:

  • Mystery over certainty.
  • Discovery over exposition.
  • Multiple perspectives.
  • Environmental storytelling.
  • Ancient history should feel distant and incomplete.

16.2.2 The Known History#

According to the teachings of the world, the Five Old Gods created civilization and have protected the world since ancient times.

The New God is believed to be a powerful divine being that seeks to corrupt creation and overthrow the natural order established by the Five Old Gods.

This version of history forms the foundation of the world's religions, myths and cultural traditions.

Most inhabitants accept it without question.

16.2.3 Forgotten History#

The true history differs significantly from the commonly accepted version.

Long before recorded history, a single divine being existed.

This being, the Ancient One, later remembered as the New God, created the Five Old Gods to govern different aspects of creation.

Over countless ages, the Old Gods grew powerful and increasingly independent.

Eventually they conspired against their creator, imprisoning him within his own subconscious and reshaping history to portray themselves as the original rulers of the world.

Almost all evidence of these events has disappeared over thousands of years.

Whether through deliberate manipulation or the passage of time remains unknown.

16.2.4 The Great Betrayal#

The imprisonment of the Ancient One marks the defining event in the history of the world.

Unable to destroy their creator, the Five Old Gods instead confined his consciousness within an endless prison formed from his own mind.

This prison ultimately became the DreamDungeon.

The physical consequences of this event on the world remain largely unknown.

Much of this history has faded into myth.

16.2.5 The Age of the Five Gods#

Following the imprisonment of their creator, the Five Old Gods became the dominant divine powers of the world.

Civilizations rose beneath their influence.

Religions were established.

Entire cultures developed around their teachings.

Over generations, the original history slowly disappeared until it survived only in fragmented legends and forgotten places.

Whether this transformation happened intentionally or naturally remains uncertain.

16.2.6 The Awakening - Early Concept#

Status: Early Concept. This possible storyline begins after countless ages, when signs appear that the Ancient One is awakening.

The DreamDungeon becomes increasingly unstable.

Strange dreams spread across the world.

Forgotten ruins begin to change.

The Five Old Gods recognize the danger and begin searching for mortals capable of entering the DreamDungeon.

This marks the beginning of the player's journey.

Lost Knowledge

Fragments of the true history remain hidden throughout the world.

Possible sources include:

  • Ancient temples.
  • Forgotten libraries.
  • Ruined civilizations.
  • Dream echoes.
  • Hidden sanctuaries.
  • Optional NPCs.
  • Environmental storytelling.

Players should never receive the complete history from a single source.

Instead, they gradually assemble their own understanding over multiple discoveries.

16.2.7 Narrative Philosophy#

The Lore should intentionally leave room for interpretation.

Not every question requires a definitive answer.

Conflicting historical records, unreliable narrators and forgotten civilizations should create uncertainty about what truly happened.

Players should finish the game understanding far more than when they began, while still debating parts of the world's history.

16.2.8 Design Goals#

The Lore aims to:

  • Create a memorable journey.
  • Support exploration.
  • Reward curiosity.
  • Create mystery.
  • Encourage discussion.
  • Strengthen replayability.
  • Provide context without overwhelming the player.

16.2.9 Open Variables#

The following topics remain under evaluation:

  • The complete creation myth.
  • Why the Ancient One created the Five Old Gods.
  • How the imprisonment was achieved.
  • How the Ancient One could escape
  • The exact passage of time.
  • The true motives of each Old God.
  • The origin of Dream Walkers.
  • How much history has been intentionally rewritten.

16.2.10 Future Expansion#

Potential future additions include:

  • Ancient civilizations.
  • Historical timelines.
  • Religious texts.
  • Legendary heroes.
  • Lost kingdoms.
  • Optional lore quests.
  • Multiple historical perspectives.

Chapter 17 — The Gods

17.1 Overview#

The Gods are the central divine beings of DreamDungeon and influence every aspect of the world, from Color Schools and biomes to Enemies, Spells, Talents and the overarching narrative. Although worshipped throughout the world, their true nature and history remain largely unknown. Each deity represents a distinct philosophy, domain and visual identity while also shaping the gameplay systems associated with its Color School.

17.2 Current Design#

17.2.1 General Concept#

The divine hierarchy currently consists of six major beings.

Five are known collectively as the Five Old Gods. The sixth is the Ancient One, remembered in modern history and World Belief as the New God.

To the people of the world, the Five Old Gods are regarded as the rightful rulers of creation.

The New God is believed to be a new Enemy to the old gods.

Whether this belief is accurate remains one of the central mysteries of DreamDungeon.

Current design principles:

  • Every god has a unique identity.
  • Every god represents one Color School.
  • Every god influences gameplay as well as narrative.
  • Gods should feel ancient and incomprehensible.
  • Their personalities should be reflected through their domains and followers.

17.2.2 The Five Old Gods#

Status: Early Concept working material. The Five Old Gods govern fundamental aspects of the world. Their domains and philosophies guide narrative development, while Chapter 7 - Color Schools remains canonical for detailed Color School gameplay identity.

Red God - God of Rage

  • Domain: Conflict, Strength, Passion, and Determination.
  • Philosophy: Power is earned through struggle; conflict drives growth and change.
  • Narrative character: Values courage above caution.
  • Gameplay connection: Red Color School; see Chapter 7 - Color Schools.

Blue God - God of Delusion

  • Domain: Knowledge, Time, and Control.
  • Philosophy: Understanding comes through patience and observation; knowledge creates order.
  • Narrative character: Seeks control through understanding.
  • Gameplay connection: Blue Color School; see Chapter 7 - Color Schools.

Green God - God of Cunning

  • Domain: Life, Adaptation, and Instinct.
  • Philosophy: Survival belongs to those who adapt; life continually changes.
  • Narrative character: Represents evolution through resilience.
  • Gameplay connection: Green Color School; see Chapter 7 - Color Schools.

Black God - God of End

  • Domain: Death, Sacrifice, and Transformation.
  • Philosophy: Nothing truly ends; death begins transformation, and power demands a price.
  • Narrative character: Embraces transformation rather than simple destruction.
  • Gameplay connection: Black Color School; see Chapter 7 - Color Schools.

White God - God of Mercy

  • Domain: Order, Protection, and Unity.
  • Philosophy: Civilization depends on cooperation; strength is measured by what one protects.
  • Narrative character: Seeks stability above all else.
  • Gameplay connection: White Color School; see Chapter 7 - Color Schools.

Their precise personalities, relationships, motivations, and gameplay expressions may be refined in future Old Gods and system Bibles.

17.2.3 The Ancient One#

To the people of the world, this being is unknowingly referred to only as the New God.

Very little reliable information exists regarding his true nature.

According to the teachings of the Five Old Gods, he seeks to dominate creation and destroy the world's balance.

Throughout the game, players gradually Encounter evidence suggesting that history may not be as straightforward as it first appears.

The Ancient One's true history is intentionally left unexplained during the early stages of the game.

17.2.4 Divine Influence#

The Gods influence nearly every major gameplay system.

Current examples include:

  • Color Schools
  • Talents
  • Altars
  • Biomes
  • Enemy factions
  • Bosses
  • Environmental storytelling

Although players rarely interact directly with the Gods, their presence should be felt throughout the entire game.

17.2.5 Worship#

Religion remains an important part of the world.

Most civilizations worship one or more of the Five Old Gods.

Different cultures may emphasize different deities depending on their traditions and environment.

The Ancient One is almost not remembered by modern people and only legends, myths and forbidden knowledge revolve around this being.

Whether these beliefs are historically accurate remains uncertain.

17.2.6 Design Goals#

The Gods should:

  • Reinforce the identity of each Color School.
  • Support gameplay systems.
  • Strengthen worldbuilding.
  • Encourage philosophical conflict.
  • Inspire mystery.
  • Avoid clear moral absolutes.

The player should eventually question whether any deity is entirely right or entirely wrong.

17.2.7 Open Variables#

The following topics remain under evaluation:

  • Individual personalities and relationships among the Gods.
  • Their precise motivations in the betrayal and current conflict.
  • Symbols, iconography, religions, and organizations.
  • How directly Gods communicate with mortals.
  • Whether and how Gods influence the physical world.

17.2.8 Future Expansion#

A possible confrontation with one or more Old Gods at the end of a future Story arc remains an uncommitted narrative idea.

Chapter 18 — Story

18.1 Overview#

The Story follows the player's journey through DreamDungeon and gradually reveals the truth hidden beneath thousands of years of forgotten history. Rather than presenting the entire narrative upfront, the story unfolds through exploration, environmental storytelling, character interactions and major discoveries throughout multiple runs.

The player begins with a simple objective, but gradually realizes that the conflict between the Gods is far more complex than it first appears.

18.2 Current Design#

18.2.1 General Concept#

The Story is structured as a gradual uncovering of truth.

Rather than focusing on numerous characters or lengthy dialogue sequences, the narrative is driven by exploration, discovery and player interpretation.

Each stage of the journey should answer previous questions while introducing new mysteries.

Current design principles:

  • Exploration drives storytelling.
  • Show rather than tell.
  • Mystery before explanation.
  • Player interpretation is encouraged.
  • The world tells its own story.
  • Rare cinematics may be considered for later story development.

18.2.2 The Beginning#

The player begins the game as an ordinary adventurer living in a world governed by the Five Old Gods.

As the influence of the New God begins spreading throughout the world, the player is chosen by the Five Old Gods to undertake a sacred mission.

The player is led to believe that they are humanity's best hope of restoring balance.

Neither the player nor the inhabitants of the world know the complete truth behind the conflict.

18.2.3 The Call#

The Five Old Gods gather the chosen adventurers within a hidden sanctuary.

Through an ancient ritual, they prepare the party for their descent into the DreamDungeon.

The exact nature of the ritual remains unknown to the player.

To them, the journey simply begins.

Only much later does the player realize that every expedition has been undertaken while their physical body remained elsewhere.

18.2.4 The Descent#

Each run represents another attempt by the same underlying Dream Walkers to descend deeper into DreamDungeon. Their run-specific Build and power reset, while persistent identity remains a separate narrative layer.

Along the way, players Encounter:

  • Ancient civilizations.
  • Forgotten temples.
  • Corrupted guardians.
  • Fragments of history.
  • Mysterious visions.
  • Bosses protecting deeper layers.

Initially, these discoveries appear unrelated.

Over time they begin forming a much larger picture.

18.2.5 The Growing Doubt#

As players descend further, inconsistencies begin to appear.

Examples may include:

  • Contradictory historical records.
  • Ancient murals.
  • Forgotten inscriptions.
  • Unexplained visions.
  • Dialogue that references previous expeditions.
  • Guardians behaving unexpectedly.

These discoveries slowly challenge everything the player believed at the beginning of the game.

The player should begin questioning not only the Dungeon, but also the motives of the Five Old Gods.

18.2.6 The Revelation#

Near the end of the story, the player finally reaches the deepest layers of the DreamDungeon.

There, the Ancient One reveals the true nature of the Dungeon and the events that led to his imprisonment.

Rather than simply explaining the past, the Ancient One confronts the player with memories and visions, allowing them to witness fragments of history firsthand.

Whether those memories represent objective truth or another form of manipulation intentionally remains uncertain.

18.2.7 The Final Choice - Future / Under Evaluation#

The player's original mission is to infuse the essence of the New God with the collective will of the Five Old Gods, trying to convey the ancient one to split his power and thereby restoring their lost power.

However, after learning the hidden history, the player begins to question whether this is truly the right course of action.

The final act should challenge the player's understanding of justice, loyalty and truth.

The exact outcome remains intentionally undecided and will be defined as the narrative develops.

18.2.8 Storytelling Methods#

DreamDungeon primarily tells its story through indirect methods.

Current storytelling techniques include:

  • Environmental storytelling.
  • Ancient murals.
  • Ruined temples.
  • Optional lore discoveries.
  • Boss Encounters.
  • Dialogue fragments of Enemies
  • Visual symbolism.
  • Rare cinematic moments.

Large exposition sequences should be avoided whenever possible.

The player should actively assemble the story rather than passively receive it.

18.2.9 Narrative Progression#

The story should progress through several distinct stages.

  • Certainty – The player believes the Five Old Gods without question.
  • Curiosity – Small inconsistencies begin to appear.
  • Doubt – The player realizes history may have been altered.
  • Discovery – The truth behind the DreamDungeon is gradually revealed.
  • Choice - Future / Under Evaluation: the player may eventually decide how to respond to what they have learned.

Each stage should naturally Build upon the previous one.

18.2.10 Design Goals#

The Story aims to:

  • Create a memorable journey.
  • Support exploration.
  • Reward curiosity.
  • Create emotional investment.
  • Encourage discussion.
  • Maintain mystery throughout the game.
  • Deliver meaningful revelations.
  • Avoid clear moral absolutes.

Players should finish the game questioning their own assumptions rather than simply defeating an antagonist.

18.2.11 Open Variables#

The following topics remain under evaluation:

  • The identity of the player before the story begins.
  • How the Five Old Gods choose Dream Walkers.
  • The exact mechanics of the dreamwalking ritual.
  • The complete ending structure.
  • Different endings (fight ancient one, fight old gods)
  • Whether players receive meaningful choices.
  • The role of other Dream Walkers.
  • How much of the Ancient One's version of history is true.

18.2.12 Future Expansion#

Potential future additions include:

  • Multiple endings.
  • Multiple alternative Final Boss fights.
  • Additional story branches.
  • Dream Walker backstories.
  • Post-game narrative content.
  • New story chapters following release.

Chapter 19 — User Experience & Presentation

19.1 Overview#

The presentation of DreamDungeon should support gameplay first and immersion second. Every visual and audio element exists to communicate gameplay information clearly while reinforcing the atmosphere of exploring an ancient and mysterious dungeon.

The user interface, visual effects, sound design and artistic direction should always prioritize readability, responsiveness and long-term usability over visual complexity.

19.2 Current Design#

19.2.1 Design Philosophy#

Presentation should enhance the gameplay rather than compete with it.

Players should always understand:

  • What is happening.
  • Why it is happening.
  • What requires immediate attention.

Visual clarity should remain the highest priority during combat.

Current design principles:

  • Gameplay before visuals.
  • Readability before realism.
  • Simplicity before complexity.
  • Timeless presentation.
  • Consistent visual language.

19.2.2 User Interface (UI)#

The user interface should remain clean, unobtrusive and easy to read.

It should communicate all important gameplay information while occupying as little screen space as possible.

Players should spend their attention on the game world rather than the interface.

Current design goals:

  • Minimalistic appearance.
  • High readability.
  • Clear information hierarchy.
  • Consistent visual language.
  • Fast information recognition.
  • Modular layout.

HUD rearrangement is partially implemented. Full rearrangement, individual element hiding, advanced Nameplate customization, and a complete HUD editor remain post-MVP unless their implementation status changes.

19.2.3 Core UI Elements#

The complete MVP interface target includes:

  • Player Unit Frame.
  • Target Unit Frame.
  • Party Frames.
  • Hotbar.
  • Resource Display.
  • Cast Bar.
  • Enemy Cast Bars.
  • Nameplates.
  • Character Sheet.
  • Inventory.
  • Spell Book.
  • Menus.
  • Tooltips.
  • Chat Window (currently implemented).

Each element should present only the information required for gameplay.

Additional information should remain accessible without cluttering the main interface.

19.2.4 Unit Frames#

Unit Frames provide detailed information about friendly and hostile units.

They should communicate:

  • Health.
  • Mana.
  • Important Conditions.
  • Cast Progress.
  • Target Selection.
  • Threat (if applicable).

Conditions displayed directly on Unit Frames may be more comprehensive than those shown within the world.

19.2.5 Nameplates#

Nameplates provide immediate combat information above units.

They should remain readable even during large Encounters.

Current priorities include:

  • Health.
  • Enemy Cast Bars.
  • High-priority Conditions.
  • Elite indicators.
  • Boss indicators.
  • Threat feedback (if implemented).

Only the most important Conditions should appear directly on Nameplates to reduce visual clutter.

19.2.6 Visual Effects (VFX)#

Visual Effects should communicate gameplay rather than overwhelm it.

Players should immediately recognize:

  • Spell origin.
  • Spell destination.
  • Area of Effect.
  • Active hazards.
  • Important Conditions.
  • Interrupt opportunities.
  • Defensive abilities.

Every Color School should possess a distinct visual identity while remaining readable during large cooperative Encounters.

Current design principles:

  • High readability.
  • Clear silhouettes.
  • Consistent Color School identity.
  • Limited particle density.
  • Immediate gameplay feedback.

Visual effects should never prevent players from understanding the battlefield.

19.2.7 Conditions#

Important Conditions use both world-space visual feedback and UI confirmation. High-priority Conditions receive the clearest combined presentation through world effects and relevant Unit Frames, Nameplates, icons, timers, or Tooltips.

Players should recognize urgent effects from the affected unit whenever possible, while the UI provides precise confirmation. Chapter 8 - Conditions owns Condition mechanics and individual implementation status.

19.2.8 Sound Effects (SFX)#

Sound effects should reinforce gameplay decisions.

Players should be able to recognize important events without constantly watching the interface.

Examples include:

  • Spell activation.
  • Successful interrupts.
  • Enemy casts.
  • Taking damage.
  • Healing received.
  • Buff activation.
  • Condition application.
  • Boss abilities.

Audio feedback should improve reaction time while strengthening immersion.

19.2.9 Music#

Music supports the emotional progression of a run.

General principles include:

  • Ambient exploration music.
  • Dynamic combat transitions (advanced adaptive behavior remains future scope).
  • Distinct biome themes.
  • Boss music.
  • Calm hub atmosphere.

As players descend deeper into the DreamDungeon, the soundtrack should gradually become darker, heavier and more unsettling.

Music should reinforce progression without distracting from gameplay.

19.2.10 Graphics#

Gameplay always takes priority over graphical fidelity.

The game should remain visually appealing while maintaining excellent gameplay readability and performance.

Current priorities:

  • Stable performance.
  • Clear silhouettes.
  • Distinct environments.
  • Consistent material language.
  • High gameplay readability.

Visual complexity should never interfere with combat clarity.

19.2.11 Art Style#

DreamDungeon aims for a timeless stylized art direction rather than photorealism.

Current inspirations include:

  • Everwild (early teaser trailers).
  • World of Warcraft.
  • Hand-painted fantasy environments.
  • Stylized low-to-mid poly assets.

Key characteristics include:

  • Strong Color School identities.
  • Distinct biome palettes.
  • Soft, painterly materials.
  • Readable silhouettes.
  • Limited visual noise.
  • Consistent environmental storytelling.

A stylized approach allows the game to age gracefully while supporting gameplay readability.

19.2.12 Accessibility#

The MVP accessibility baseline includes:

  • Key rebinding.
  • Essential audio controls.
  • Readable text.
  • Scalable core UI.
  • Communication that does not rely on Color alone.

Advanced accessibility, subtitle options, specialized modes, adjustable combat text, individual interface toggles, and a complete customizable HUD remain later development unless their implementation status changes. Accessibility should improve usability without changing gameplay balance.

19.2.13 Design Goals#

The presentation aims to:

  • Support gameplay through clarity.
  • Reinforce immersion.
  • Maintain consistent visual identity.
  • Minimize visual clutter.
  • Communicate gameplay efficiently.
  • Age gracefully through a stylized art direction.

19.2.14 Open Variables#

The following systems remain under evaluation:

  • Full UI customization beyond the partially implemented rearrangement.
  • Advanced Nameplate customization.
  • Combat text options.
  • Dynamic camera effects.
  • Controller interface and navigation.
  • Advanced accessibility and specialized modes beyond the MVP baseline.

19.2.15 Future Expansion#

Potential future additions include:

  • Advanced HUD editor.
  • Additional accessibility options.
  • Expanded settings.
  • UI add-ons, mod support, and public extension APIs.

These are Future Ideas, not Technical GDD requirements.

Appendix A - AI-Assisted Maintenance and Synchronization

◆

A.1 Purpose

This appendix defines how future human editors, developers, and AI agents may maintain the Technical GDD without allowing its Word, Markdown, HTML, or System Bible editions to diverge. It is maintenance guidance, not game design.

A.2 Canonical Sources and Priority

When sources conflict, use the following order:

  1. New explicit designer decisions.
  2. Approved Technical GDD decisions and change records.
  3. The current approved Technical GDD Markdown content.
  4. Game Philosophy Bible v0.3 for design philosophy.
  5. Approved terminology and design-freeze records.
  6. System Bibles for individual records and values within their declared ownership.
  7. Protected Technical GDD 1.0 and older sources.

The protected GDD 1.0 is historical source evidence and must never be edited or overwritten.

A.3 Document Ownership

  • The Technical GDD owns shared system rules, schemas, relationships, and current final-game design.
  • The Game Philosophy Bible owns design philosophy and nontechnical principles.
  • System Bibles own individual Spells, Conditions, Enemies, Encounters, Bosses, Equipment, Talents, and other records once those Bibles exist and have been verified.
  • The glossary owns canonical term definitions, aliases, categories, and filtering metadata.
  • Structured lists or spreadsheets own bulk implementation records when explicitly named by the GDD.

Detailed content remains in the GDD until a destination Bible exists and migration has been verified.

A.4 Safe Automatic Changes

An editor or AI agent may automatically:

  • Fix spelling, grammar, formatting, and broken internal links without changing design meaning.
  • Update synchronized Word and HTML editions from approved Markdown.
  • Replace exact or semantic duplicates with one canonical definition and a cross-reference.
  • Apply an explicitly approved terminology change consistently.
  • Update tables of contents, version metadata, and changelog entries.

A.5 Changes Requiring Designer Review

Escalate before changing:

  • Gameplay behavior, values, scope, ownership, or implementation status.
  • Final-game versus MVP classification.
  • Open Variables or Future Expansion status.
  • Narrative truth, perspective, or revelation order.
  • Similar passages whose chapter context may give them different purposes.
  • A reusable schema when a System Bible introduces a genuinely new field or behavior.

A.6 Synchronization Workflow

  1. Record the proposed change and its owner.
  2. Update the canonical Markdown content.
  3. Review affected cross-references, glossary terms, schemas, and System Bibles.
  4. Regenerate Word and HTML from the approved Markdown.
  5. Validate headings, links, tables, version identifiers, and downloadable files.
  6. Add a changelog entry describing what changed, why, and which documents were synchronized.
  7. Preserve the superseded approved edition in the project archive.

A.7 Changelog Requirements

Every approved release records:

  • Version and date.
  • Author or approving designer.
  • Changed chapters or systems.
  • Added, changed, moved, or removed information.
  • Affected System Bibles, glossary entries, and structured files.
  • Open Variables resolved or newly created.
  • Required developer follow-up.

A.8 Terminology

Use Combat Area, except for the MVP-only Combat Arena. The final hierarchy is Act -> Dungeon -> Section. Use Tank, not Frontliner. Use Equipment for equippable Items and Item for the broader category. Ancient One expresses Objective Truth; New God expresses World Belief or early Player Knowledge.

When terminology changes, update the glossary, headings, internal links, System Bibles, and implementation-facing structured records together.
